## S1 — What is Ethics? · Ethics in the Business World

**Hook:** "Your friend shares paid Netflix/Canva login with 8 people, an officer takes 'chiya kharcha' to speed a file, a company dumps e-waste in the river — *legal*? *ethical*? Are those the same question?" → ethics vs law.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Ethics, morals, law, etiquette**
   - Definition: ethics = a code of behavior defining right/wrong for a group/society.
   - Theory: morals (personal beliefs) vs ethics (group/professional code) vs law (enforced rules) vs etiquette (manners).
   - Global example: something legal but unethical (exploiting a loophole) vs illegal but seen as ethical by some.
   - Local example: paying a bribe ("ghus") is illegal *and* unethical; cutting a queue is unethical but not illegal.
   - Misconception: *"If it's legal, it's ethical."* Correction: law is the floor, not the ceiling.

2. **Ethics in the business world**
   - Definition: applying ethical standards to business conduct.
   - Theory: stakeholders (customers, employees, society, shareholders); short-term profit vs long-term trust.
   - Local example: a Nepali company over-charging during a festival shortage vs one that holds prices.
   - Mini case: "A trekking agency hides a safety risk to keep a booking — discuss."

3. **Why businesses behave unethically**
   - Pressure for results, "everyone does it," weak oversight.
   - Fun element: analogy — ethics is the brakes that let a business drive fast *safely*, not the thing that slows it down.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: An action that is legal but still wrong is best called…? → unethical ✅
- MCQ2: A professional code of conduct is an example of…? → ethics (group code) ✅
- Discussion: "Name one thing that's legal in Nepal but you consider unethical."

**Real-life application:** every job offer you sign will have a code of conduct — and your online reputation is built on ethical choices employers now check.

**Summary:** (1) ethics = group code of right/wrong; (2) law ≠ ethics; (3) business ethics balances profit and trust. **Next:** Corporate Social Responsibility.

**Visual cues:** Venn diagram Law vs Ethics vs Morals; "legal–illegal × ethical–unethical" 2×2 grid with examples.

## S2 — Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) · Fostering CSR & Good Business Ethics

**Hook:** "When Ncell funds a school or a bank plants trees — is that kindness, marketing, or duty?" → CSR.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)**
   - Definition: a company's duty to act for the benefit of society, not just shareholders.
   - Theory: economic, legal, ethical, philanthropic responsibilities (Carroll's pyramid).
   - Local example: Nepali banks' mandatory CSR spend (NRB directive); telecom rural-coverage programs.
   - Misconception: *"CSR is just charity/PR."* Correction: it's embedded responsibility, not only donations.

2. **Stakeholder theory**
   - Definition: a business is accountable to all stakeholders, not only owners.
   - Mini case: a factory profitable for owners but polluting a village's water — who counts?

3. **Fostering CSR & good business ethics**
   - Tone from the top, a code of ethics, ethical culture, social-responsibility reporting.
   - Fun element: "good ethics is good business" — trust compounds like interest.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: Carroll's CSR pyramid base is…? → economic responsibility ✅
- MCQ2: Stakeholders include all EXCEPT…? → only shareholders ✅
- Discussion: "Name a Nepali company doing visible CSR — is it genuine or image-building?"

**Real-life application:** CSR shapes which companies attract talent and investment — and which face boycotts.

**Summary:** (1) CSR = duty to society; (2) stakeholders > shareholders alone; (3) culture + code foster it. **Next:** how firms *improve* business ethics.

**Visual cues:** Carroll's CSR pyramid (4 layers); stakeholder map (company at center, arrows to groups).

## S3 — Improving Business Ethics

**Hook:** "A company writes a beautiful 'Code of Ethics,' frames it on the wall — and nothing changes. Why?" → ethics needs systems, not posters.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Why ethics programs fail or succeed**
   - Definition: an ethics program = the systems that turn values into behavior.
   - Theory: leadership example, training, reporting channels, accountability.

2. **Components of improving business ethics**
   - Appoint a corporate ethics officer; board oversight; code of conduct; ethics training; safe reporting (whistle-blower) channels; audits.
   - Local example: a Nepali bank's compliance/AML training and grievance hotline.
   - Misconception: *"A code of conduct alone guarantees ethical behavior."* Correction: enforcement + culture matter more than the document.

3. **Measuring & sustaining ethics**
   - Ethics audits, surveys, consequences for violations.
   - Mini case: "Two employees break the same rule; only the junior is punished — what does that teach everyone?"

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: The most important driver of an ethical culture is…? → leadership/tone at the top ✅
- MCQ2: A safe channel to report wrongdoing is a…? → whistle-blower/reporting hotline ✅
- Discussion: "Design one rule that would make your college's exam process more ethical."

**Real-life application:** knowing how real ethics programs work helps you spot a healthy vs toxic employer.

**Summary:** (1) values need systems; (2) leadership + training + reporting + audits; (3) enforcement beats posters. **Next:** a step-by-step decision-making process.

**Visual cues:** "ethics program" flow (Code → Training → Reporting → Audit → Consequences) as a cycle.

## S4 — Ethical Considerations in Decision Making

**Hook:** "You find a classmate's leaked exam answers in a shared drive the night before the exam. Walk through what you actually do — and *how* you decide." → a decision framework.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **A structured ethical decision-making process**
   - Steps: get the facts → identify stakeholders & options → evaluate using ethical lenses → choose & act → reflect on the outcome.
   - Theory: this is the spine students reuse all semester.

2. **Ethical lenses / philosophies (applied, not abstract)**
   - Utilitarian (greatest good), rights/duty (deontology), fairness/justice, common good, virtue.
   - Local example: a load-shedding-era fuel queue — how each lens judges "jumping the line for an ambulance."
   - Misconception: *"Ethics is just personal opinion."* Correction: structured reasoning gives defensible, comparable answers.

3. **Common decision traps**
   - Rationalization, groupthink, "slippery slope," obeying authority blindly.
   - Mini case: a manager tells you to fudge one report "just this once."

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: The first step in ethical decision making is…? → gather the facts ✅
- MCQ2: "Greatest good for the greatest number" is which lens? → utilitarian ✅
- Discussion: "Apply the 5 steps to the leaked-answers scenario."

**Real-life application:** you'll face grey-area calls in every workplace; a repeatable process protects you and others.

**Summary:** (1) a 5-step process; (2) multiple ethical lenses; (3) beware decision traps. **Next:** why all of this matters specifically in IT.

**Visual cues:** 5-step decision flowchart; table of the 5 ethical lenses with one-line tests.

## S5 — Ethics in Information Technology (closes Unit 1)

**Hook:** "An app quietly sells your location data, an algorithm rejects loan applicants by neighborhood, a deepfake of a politician goes viral before an election — IT makes ethical mistakes *scale to millions instantly*." → why IT ethics is special.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Why IT raises distinct ethical challenges**
   - Scale, speed, anonymity, permanence of data, and the gap between capability and regulation.
   - Theory: technology is not neutral — design choices encode values.

2. **Core IT-ethics themes (preview of the whole course)**
   - Privacy & data protection, intellectual property, security, accuracy, access (the "PAPA" framing).
   - Local example: misuse of personal data leaked from a Nepali service; viral misinformation on social media during elections/disasters.
   - Misconception: *"It's just technology, the tool is neutral."* Correction: choices about what to build and how encode ethics.

3. **The IT professional's responsibility**
   - "Can vs should"; professional codes (ACM/IEEE); duty to users and the public.
   - Mini case: "You can scrape 100,000 users' public data for a class project — should you?"

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: Which makes IT ethics distinct? → scale/speed/permanence of impact ✅
- MCQ2: "PAPA" in IT ethics stands for Privacy, Accuracy, Property, and…? → Access ✅
- Discussion: "Name one IT product you use that raises an ethical concern, and why."

**Real-life application:** as future BIM graduates building/managing systems, *you* will make these calls — this course is your toolkit.

**Summary:** (1) IT scales ethical impact; (2) tech is value-laden; (3) professionals own "can vs should." **Next unit:** ethics specifically for IT workers and users.

**Visual cues:** "PAPA" four-quadrant graphic; a "can vs should" balance scale.
