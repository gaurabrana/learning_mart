## S6 — Managing the IT Worker Relationship

**Hook:** "A vendor offers the bank's IT manager a 'free' iPhone the week before a Rs 2-crore tender decision; a developer moonlights for a client's competitor on weekends — who is each person *actually* loyal to?" → an IT worker juggles many relationships at once.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Why IT workers face special relationship pressures**
   - Definition: an IT worker = a professional who designs, builds, maintains, or manages IT systems and the data inside them.
   - Theory: IT workers hold privileged access (admin rights, personal data, source code) and high-trust positions, so each relationship carries a duty of care.
   - Global example: a sysadmin abusing root access to read executives' emails.
   - Local example: a bank IT staffer with database access could view any customer's balance — trust, not just permission, governs the relationship.
   - Misconception: *"IT is just a back-office technical job, ethics is for managers."* Correction: access = power = responsibility.

2. **Relationship with employers**
   - Definition: the employee's duty of loyalty, honesty, and protecting company assets/IP.
   - Theory: conflicts of interest, whistle-blowing tension, resume fraud, software-license compliance.
   - Local example: a Kathmandu IT firm staffer copying client source code to use at a side gig.
   - Mini case: "Your employer asks you to install pirated Microsoft/AutoCAD on office machines to cut cost — what do you do?"

3. **Relationship with clients**
   - Definition: a fiduciary/professional duty — give honest advice, deliver what was promised, disclose risks.
   - Theory: information asymmetry (the client can't judge technical quality), conflict of interest, breach of contract.
   - Local example: a software vendor over-promising an e-governance module to a municipality, then under-delivering.
   - Misconception: *"If the contract doesn't ban it, it's fine."* Correction: professional duty goes beyond the contract's letter.

4. **Relationship with suppliers, other professionals, IT users, and society**
   - Suppliers: bribery, kickbacks, fair dealing (the "free iPhone" hook).
   - Other professionals: respect, no resume inflation, no poaching trade secrets, professional courtesy.
   - IT users: support, training, not exploiting their lack of skill.
   - Society: building safe systems, not enabling harm (e.g., a system that leaks citizen data).
   - Fun element: analogy — an IT worker is like a hospital pharmacist: trusted with powerful "medicine" (access/data) that helps or harms depending on the hand.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: A supplier gifting an IT manager a phone before a tender is best described as…? → a conflict of interest / bribery ✅
- MCQ2: The IT worker's duty to give honest technical advice applies most directly to the…? → client ✅
- Discussion: "Which relationship do you think is hardest for a junior IT worker in Nepal to manage honestly — and why?"

**Real-life application:** your first IT job will hand you access and vendor contacts on day one; how you handle gifts, access, and honesty defines your reputation early.

**Summary:** (1) IT workers manage 6 key relationships; (2) privileged access creates duties; (3) loyalty, honesty, and no-conflict-of-interest run through all of them. **Next:** how the profession itself encourages good behavior.

**Visual cues:** "IT worker at the center" relationship web (6 arrows: employer, client, supplier, peers, users, society); a "gift vs bribe" line graphic.

## S7 — Encouraging Professionalism of IT Workers

**Hook:** "A doctor swears an oath and can lose a license; an engineer signs off on a bridge. But anyone can call themselves an 'IT professional' tomorrow — should IT be regulated the same way?" → what makes IT a *profession*.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Is IT a 'profession'?**
   - Definition: a profession = a field requiring specialized education, with a body of knowledge, a code of ethics, and self-regulation.
   - Theory: IT partly meets this (knowledge, codes) but lacks universal licensing — debated status.
   - Local example: in Nepal, doctors (NMC) and engineers (NEC) are licensed; IT workers generally are not.
   - Misconception: *"Anyone who fixes computers is an IT professional."* Correction: professionalism is about standards and accountability, not just skill.

2. **Professional codes of ethics**
   - Definition: a published set of principles members agree to uphold (e.g., ACM Code of Ethics, IEEE-CS).
   - Theory: codes set expectations, build public trust, offer decision guidance, and a basis for discipline.
   - Local example: a Nepali software company adopting an internal code modeled on ACM; CAN (Computer Association of Nepal) community norms.
   - Mini case: "An ACM principle says 'avoid harm' — apply it to a developer asked to build a hidden tracking feature."

3. **Certification**
   - Definition: a vendor/industry credential proving a specific skill (CCNA, CISSP, CompTIA, AWS, PMP).
   - Theory: signals competence to employers; voluntary; can become outdated; not the same as a license.
   - Local example: Nepali IT graduates pursuing CCNA/CEH to compete for jobs and remote/outsourcing work.
   - Misconception: *"Certification = guaranteed ethical behavior."* Correction: it proves skill, not integrity.

4. **Licensing**
   - Definition: government-granted permission to practice, with legal force.
   - Theory: protects the public in high-stakes fields; debate on whether software engineering needs it (e.g., safety-critical systems).
   - Global example: "Professional Engineer (PE) — Software" licensing debate in the US.

5. **Professional organizations**
   - Definition: bodies that set standards, run certifications, publish codes, and represent members (ACM, IEEE-CS, ISACA).
   - Local example: Computer Association of Nepal (CAN), NITP / IT Professional Forum, NIC; their role in advocacy and training.
   - Fun element: analogy — a code of ethics is the "terms of service" you accept to be trusted as a professional.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: A government-granted, legally enforced permission to practice is…? → licensing ✅
- MCQ2: ACM and IEEE-CS are examples of…? → professional organizations (with codes of ethics) ✅
- Discussion: "Should IT workers in Nepal be licensed like engineers and doctors? Argue one side."

**Real-life application:** employers and outsourcing clients increasingly ask for certifications + adherence to a code of conduct; these shape your hireability and pay.

**Summary:** (1) IT is a partial profession; (2) codes guide behavior; (3) certification proves skill, licensing has legal force; (4) professional bodies set the standards. **Next:** turning to the *users* — how to keep IT use ethical.

**Visual cues:** comparison table (Certification vs Licensing: who grants, legal force, voluntary?); logos/list of bodies (ACM, IEEE-CS, ISACA, CAN-Nepal).

## S8 — Encouraging Ethical Use of IT Resources among Users

**Hook:** "Your office Wi-Fi password is shared with the whole team — someone uses it to torrent movies, another installs a cracked Photoshop, a third runs a part-time crypto miner on the server overnight. Whose problem is it?" → users need rules, not just tools.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Who is an 'IT user' and why their behavior matters**
   - Definition: an IT user = anyone who uses an organization's IT resources (staff, students, customers).
   - Theory: most security and ethics incidents start with ordinary users, not hackers; the "insider" risk.
   - Local example: a college lab where students install games/cracked software on shared PCs.
   - Misconception: *"Only the IT department is responsible for misuse."* Correction: every user shares responsibility.

2. **Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)**
   - Definition: a document stating what users may and may not do with the organization's IT resources.
   - Theory: defines permitted use, prohibited use, monitoring, and consequences; users sign/acknowledge it.
   - Local example: a Nepali bank or telecom (NTC/Ncell) staff AUP banning personal USB drives and unlicensed software; a university IT-use policy.
   - Mini case: "Draft 3 rules you'd put in your college lab's AUP."

3. **Common ethical issue: software piracy**
   - Definition: copying, installing, or distributing software without a valid license.
   - Theory: types — counterfeiting, unlicensed installs, license violation; legal + ethical harm; links to Unit 3 (IP).
   - Local example: high software-piracy rates in Nepal/South Asia; offices running cracked Windows/Office; "everyone does it" normalization.
   - Misconception: *"It's only piracy if I sell it."* Correction: using an unlicensed copy is already a violation.
   - Fun element: analogy — using cracked software is like sneaking into a movie hall: cheaper today, but you're not entitled to it.

4. **Common ethical issue: inappropriate use of IT resources**
   - Definition: using org resources for unauthorized, personal, or harmful purposes.
   - Theory: examples — excessive personal browsing, streaming, gaming, accessing offensive content, using company email for side business, installing unauthorized apps.
   - Local example: a government office computer used for personal Facebook/online shopping during work hours.
   - Mini case: "Is checking eSewa/Khalti and replying to a family message at work 'inappropriate use'? Where's the line?"

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: The document that defines what users may/may not do with IT resources is the…? → Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) ✅
- MCQ2: Installing a cracked copy of Office for personal office work is…? → software piracy (even without selling it) ✅
- Discussion: "Is software piracy in Nepal mainly an ethics problem, an affordability problem, or an enforcement problem?"

**Real-life application:** on day one of any job you'll sign an AUP; breaking it (piracy, misuse) is a common reason people get fired or sued.

**Summary:** (1) users cause most incidents; (2) AUPs set the rules; (3) piracy and inappropriate use are the two biggest user ethics issues. **Next:** the user's flip side — their privacy and anonymity.

**Visual cues:** AUP "allowed vs prohibited" two-column card; software-piracy types diagram; Nepal/South Asia piracy-rate bar (illustrative).

## S9 — Key Privacy and Anonymity Issues

**Hook:** "You fill a loan form at a bank, give your number to a SIM dealer, scan a QR to enter a mall — and a week later you get spam calls about loans you never asked for. Where did your data go, and did you ever agree?" → privacy in a data-driven world.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Data privacy**
   - Definition: the right of individuals to control how their personal data is collected, used, and shared.
   - Theory: personal data, consent, purpose limitation, data minimization; harms from misuse (profiling, fraud).
   - Local example: KYC data at Nepali banks/telecoms; the Nepal *Individual Privacy Act, 2075 (2018)*; leaks of citizen data.
   - Misconception: *"I have nothing to hide, so privacy doesn't matter to me."* Correction: privacy is about control and power, not hiding wrongdoing.

2. **Surveillance**
   - Definition: systematic monitoring of people's activities, communications, or location.
   - Theory: workplace monitoring, CCTV, government surveillance, app/location tracking; security-vs-privacy tradeoff.
   - Local example: rising CCTV in Kathmandu; employers monitoring staff email/screens; SIM/biometric registration linking identity to activity.
   - Mini case: "Your employer installs software that logs every keystroke and screenshot. Ethical? Where's the limit?"

3. **Anonymity online**
   - Definition: acting online without revealing your real identity.
   - Theory: the double edge — protects whistle-blowers, activists, and free speech, but also shields trolls, scammers, and criminals.
   - Local example: anonymous Nepali Facebook/Twitter (X) pages exposing corruption vs anonymous pages spreading rumors/harassment.
   - Misconception: *"Online I'm completely anonymous."* Correction: IP logs, metadata, and device fingerprints make true anonymity rare.

4. **Identity (and identity theft)**
   - Definition: the data that represents you online (name, citizenship no., bank/eSewa logins, biometrics).
   - Theory: identity theft, impersonation, fake profiles; phishing as the entry point (preview of Unit 7).
   - Local example: fake Facebook profiles impersonating Nepali public figures; phishing SMS posing as eSewa/bank to steal OTPs.
   - Fun element: analogy — your personal data is like your house key: copying it is easy, and you rarely notice until someone's already inside.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: "Collect only the data you actually need" is the principle of…? → data minimization ✅
- MCQ2: Online anonymity is best described as…? → a double-edged tool (protects and enables harm) ✅
- Discussion: "Should the government be allowed to require real-name registration to post on social media? Trade-offs?"

**Real-life application:** as a BIM graduate you'll handle customer data; mishandling it can break Nepal's privacy law and destroy customer trust.

**Summary:** (1) privacy = control over your data; (2) surveillance trades security for privacy; (3) anonymity cuts both ways; (4) identity is the new target. **Next:** where privacy, anonymity, and harm collide most — social networking.

**Visual cues:** "data trail" flow (you → form/QR/SIM → company → third parties); security-vs-privacy seesaw; anonymity double-edged-sword icon.

## S10 — Social Networking Ethical Issues (closes Unit 2)

**Hook:** "A doctored screenshot of a Nepali celebrity goes viral on TikTok/Facebook before lunch; by evening a teenager is being mocked in her school group chat, and a 'breaking news' rumor sparks a panic. Nobody broke into a server — people just used the apps." → social media weaponizes everyday users.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Why social networking is an ethics hotspot**
   - Definition: social networking = platforms where users create and share content and connect (Facebook, TikTok, Instagram, X, YouTube).
   - Theory: scale + speed + anonymity + virality make small acts cause large harm; the "bystander" effect online.
   - Local example: Nepal's very high Facebook/TikTok usage; viral content shaping public opinion and elections.
   - Misconception: *"It's just online, it isn't real / it doesn't count."* Correction: online harm has real legal, social, and psychological consequences.

2. **Cyberbullying and online harassment**
   - Definition: using digital tools to repeatedly threaten, humiliate, or target a person.
   - Theory: forms — trolling, doxxing, group pile-ons, harassment of women online; mental-health impact.
   - Local example: harassment of Nepali women/public figures on Facebook/TikTok comment sections; school group-chat bullying.
   - Mini case: "Your friend group starts mocking a classmate in a shared chat. You stay silent — are you complicit?"

3. **Fake news and misinformation**
   - Definition: false or misleading information spread (deliberately = disinformation; carelessly = misinformation).
   - Theory: why it spreads (emotion, confirmation bias, engagement algorithms); ethics of sharing without verifying.
   - Local example: false earthquake/disaster rumors, fake health cures, and election misinformation circulated on Nepali social media.
   - Misconception: *"Sharing isn't the same as creating it, so I'm not responsible."* Correction: forwarding unverified content spreads the harm.
   - Fun element: analogy — a lie online travels like a forwarded festival greeting: cheap to send, impossible to recall.

4. **Defamation and online reputation harm**
   - Definition: a false statement presented as fact that damages someone's reputation.
   - Theory: defamation can be a cybercrime in Nepal (Electronic Transactions Act, 2063 — preview of Unit 9); difference between opinion/criticism and defamation.
   - Local example: Nepali cases where people were arrested under the ETA for defamatory Facebook posts; debate over free speech vs misuse of the law.
   - Mini case: "You post 'I think shop X overcharges' vs 'Shop X is a fraud that steals money' — which risks defamation, and why?"

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: Deliberately spreading false information to mislead is…? → disinformation ✅
- MCQ2: A false factual statement that damages someone's reputation is…? → defamation ✅
- Discussion: "Before you share a shocking post, what 2 checks should you do? Would they have stopped a recent Nepali viral rumor?"

**Real-life application:** your public posts are part of your professional reputation; in Nepal, a careless defamatory or harassing post can also lead to arrest under the ETA.

**Summary:** (1) social media scales everyday acts into big harm; (2) cyberbullying/harassment, (3) fake news, and (4) defamation are the core issues — verify before you share. **Next unit:** Intellectual Property — copyright, patents, trade secrets, and IP misuse.

**Visual cues:** "share/don't share" verification flowchart; spectrum graphic: opinion → criticism → defamation; virality "1 post → thousands" spread diagram.
