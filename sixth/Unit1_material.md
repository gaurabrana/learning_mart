## Unit 1 — Learning Outcomes
By the end of this unit, students will be able to:
1. Define *ethics* and distinguish it from morals, law, and etiquette.
2. Explain ethics in the business world and the idea of *Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)*.
3. Describe how organizations foster CSR and improve business ethics.
4. Apply a structured *ethical decision-making* process to a dilemma.
5. Explain why ethics matters specifically in *Information Technology*.

# S1 — What is Ethics? · Ethics in the Business World
**Lecture hour 1 of 5 · 50 minutes**

### 🎯 OPENING — Hook `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **"Legal? Ethical? Are those the same question?"**

> **Deliver (≈2 min):** Put three short scenarios on screen and read them slowly:
> 1. Your friend shares **one paid Netflix/Canva login** with 8 people.
> 2. An office worker accepts **"chiya kharcha"** to push a file to the top of the pile.
> 3. A company quietly **dumps its e-waste in the river** at night.
>
> **Run a quick vote (≈3 min):** "Hands up — which of these are **illegal**?" Count. "Now — which
> are **unethical**?" Count. The two counts won't match, and that mismatch *is* today's lesson:
> **legal and ethical are different questions.** Tell them: by the end of this unit you'll have a
> tool to reason about exactly these grey areas.

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Don't resolve the scenarios yet — the point is to surface disagreement. If the
> class is quiet, cold-call: "You voted illegal but not unethical — say why." Write the day's
> agenda on the board: ethics vs law → ethics in business → why good companies still go wrong.

---

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — Ethics, Morals, Law & Etiquette `[THEORY]` `[~8 min]`

[SLIDE] **Four words people mix up**

> **Deliver:** Write four words on the board: **ETHICS · MORALS · LAW · ETIQUETTE.** Most people
> use them interchangeably; a professional shouldn't. Define each, and — more importantly —
> explain how they *relate*:
>
> - **Ethics** is a code of right and wrong that belongs to a **group, profession, or society.**
>   It's *shared*. That's why your college can have a code of conduct, the ACM can have a different
>   one, and Nepali society can have its own norms — all at once.
> - **Morals** are an **individual's personal** beliefs about right and wrong, often from family,
>   religion, or upbringing. Two ethical professionals can have different personal morals.
> - **Law** is the set of rules the **state enforces**, with real penalties. Law is *codified* and
>   *backed by force* — but it's slow to change and can't cover every situation.
> - **Etiquette** is **social manners** — eating with the right hand, addressing elders politely.
>   Breaking it is rude, not wrong in a deep sense.
>
> **The key relationship to land:** law is what you *must* do; ethics is what you *should* do;
> morals are what *you personally* believe; etiquette is what's *polite*. They usually overlap —
> but the cases that matter are where they **pull apart.**

- 🌍 **Global example:** exploiting a tax loophole is often perfectly *legal* yet widely judged *unethical*.
- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** paying a bribe ("ghus") is **illegal *and* unethical**; cutting a line at a
  government office using "source-force" is **unethical but not illegal**; eating with your left
  hand at a feast is only an **etiquette** breach.

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"If it's legal, it's ethical."*
> ✅ **Correction — say this out loud:** "The law is the **floor, not the ceiling.** It's the
> *minimum* society will tolerate, not the *maximum* you should aspire to. History is full of
> things that were legal and deeply wrong — so 'it's legal' can never be the end of an ethical
> conversation."

> 🎙️ Speaker note (transition): "If legal and ethical aren't the same, we need a way to *see* the
> difference. That's the grid on the next slide."

#### Concept 2 — The Legal/Ethical Grid (worked example) `[EXAMPLE]` `[~6 min]`

[SLIDE] **Two axes, four quadrants**

> 🖼️ Visual: a 2×2 grid — axis 1 *Legal ↔ Illegal*, axis 2 *Ethical ↔ Unethical*.

> **Deliver — fill the grid live, asking the class for each cell:**
> - **Legal + Ethical** (top-left): paying your taxes honestly; keeping a promise to a customer. *The easy quadrant.*
> - **Legal + Unethical** (top-right): the tax loophole; queue-jumping via connections. **← stress this one:** "Most business and IT scandals live *here* — technically allowed, clearly wrong. This is where you'll spend your career making judgment calls."
> - **Illegal + Ethical** (bottom-left): rare and debated — e.g. a justified act of civil disobedience, or a whistle-blower breaking an NDA to expose fraud.
> - **Illegal + Unethical** (bottom-right): paying a bribe; leaking someone's private data. *The obvious-wrong quadrant.*

> **Worked example:** take scenario 2 from the hook (the "chiya kharcha" bribe) and place it
> together — bottom-right. Then take the shared streaming login — ask the class which quadrant.
> (It's arguably legal-but-unethical / a grey contract violation — let them argue.)

> 🎙️ Speaker note: The takeaway students must leave with: "Ethics is the harder question, and the
> dangerous quadrant is legal-but-unethical, because nothing stops you except your own judgment."

#### Concept 3 — Ethics in the Business World `[THEORY]` `[~8 min]`

[SLIDE] **Why business ethics is its own topic**

> **Deliver:** Define **business ethics** = applying ethical standards to commercial conduct and
> decisions. Then explain *why business is special*: a business serves **multiple stakeholders at
> once** — customers, employees, society, and shareholders — and their interests **conflict.** What
> maximizes this quarter's profit can harm customers or staff. So business ethics is fundamentally
> about **managing that conflict**, and the central tension is always **short-term profit vs
> long-term trust.**
>
> Explain the trust mechanism concretely: a customer can't verify everything a business tells them
> (does this medicine work? is this "organic"? is my data safe?). Business runs on **trust**, and
> trust is **slow to build, instant to lose.** Unethical shortcuts spend down a reserve that took
> years to fill.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example (walk through it):** during a festival shortage, **Shop A triples its prices**
  to maximize profit; **Shop B holds prices.** Shop A wins today. But customers remember who
  exploited them and who didn't — and Shop B's queue is longer every Dashain after. *Good ethics
  here is also good business, just on a longer clock.*

> **Mini case (pose, take 2–3 answers, ≈2 min):** A trekking agency knows a route has a current
> safety risk but **hides it to keep a lucrative booking.** Ask: "Who bears the cost they're
> ignoring?" Draw out: the client (safety), the agency (reputation/lawsuits), and the *whole Nepali
> tourism sector* (one accident scares off thousands of future tourists). One agency's shortcut is
> an industry's loss.

#### Concept 4 — Why Good People & Businesses Still Go Wrong `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **The usual pressures**

> **Deliver:** Students assume unethical behavior comes from "bad people." Correct this: mostly it
> comes from **ordinary people under predictable pressures.** Name and explain each:
> - **Results pressure** — "hit the target or you're out." The target crowds out the ethics.
> - **"Everyone does it"** — normalization. If piracy/bribery is everywhere, it stops feeling wrong.
> - **Weak oversight** — no one is watching or accountable, so the cost of cutting corners feels zero.
> - **Diffusion of responsibility** — "I just followed the process / my manager decided."
>
> The reason this matters: if unethical behavior is *situational*, then **good systems and culture
> prevent it** — which is exactly what S2 and S3 are about. You don't just hire "good people"; you
> build conditions where good behavior is the easy default.

> 🍿 **Fun analogy (deliver it):** "People think ethics is the *brakes* that slow a business down.
> Wrong. Brakes are exactly what let a car drive **fast safely** — take the brakes off and you
> can't speed, you can only crash. A business with strong ethics can move aggressively *because* it
> won't destroy its own reputation doing so."

#### 🛠 ACTIVITY — "Find the grey area" `[ACTIVITY]` `[~6 min]`

[SLIDE] **Think–Pair–Share**

> **Run it:** In pairs (2 min), each pair invents **one act that is legal but unethical** and **one
> that is illegal but arguably ethical**, drawn from Nepali daily life or IT. Then take **4–5
> answers aloud** (3 min) and place each on the grid from Concept 2. Close (1 min): "Notice how
> easy it was to find legal-but-unethical examples — that's the quadrant this whole course trains
> you to handle."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Good seeds if a pair is stuck — selling user data that you legally collected;
> a politician's legal-but-unethical conflict of interest; pirating software because it's
> unaffordable (illegal, students will argue ethical).

---

### 🧠 CHECK FOR UNDERSTANDING `[QUIZ]` `[~5 min]`

**MCQ 1.** An action that is **legal but still wrong** is best described as:
a) illegal b) ✅ **unethical** c) good etiquette d) a moral duty

**MCQ 2.** A profession's written code of conduct is an example of:
a) personal morals b) etiquette c) ✅ **ethics (a group code)** d) law

**Discussion prompt:** *Name one thing legal in Nepal that you personally consider unethical — and say which quadrant of the grid it sits in.*

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Expect queue-jumping with connections, "source-force," legal-but-polluting
> practices. For each, confirm the quadrant on the board.

---

### 💡 REAL-LIFE APPLICATION `[~3 min]`
**This matters because…** every job offer you sign comes with a **code of conduct**, and employers
increasingly check your **online reputation** before hiring. Tell them plainly: your ethical
choices — online and offline — are part of your professional record *before* you walk into the
interview. The grey-area judgment you practiced today is a skill recruiters actively test in
behavioral interviews ("tell me about a time you faced an ethical dilemma").

---

### 📝 SUMMARY & TAKEAWAYS `[~2 min]`
1. **Ethics** = a group's code of right/wrong; distinct from morals, law, and etiquette.
2. **Legal ≠ ethical** — the law is the floor, not the ceiling; watch the legal-but-unethical quadrant.
3. **Business ethics** trades short-term profit against long-term trust; misbehavior is usually situational, so **systems and culture** are the fix.

# S2 — Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) · Fostering CSR & Good Business Ethics
**Lecture hour 2 of 5 · 50 minutes**

### 🎯 OPENING — Hook `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **"Kindness, marketing, or duty?"**

> **Deliver (≈2 min):** "When **Ncell funds a rural school**, or a commercial bank runs a
> **tree-planting drive**, or a company sponsors **flood relief** — is that generosity, clever
> marketing, or an actual responsibility they *owe* society?" Take a quick show of hands for each
> of the three.
>
> **Set up (≈3 min):** Most students vote "marketing" cynically. Acknowledge it — then complicate
> it: "What if it's *all three at once*, and what if society can legitimately *expect* it? Today we
> give that expectation a name — **CSR** — and we'll see Nepal actually *requires* some of it."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Keep the cynicism alive as a thread — we'll test "genuine vs image-building" in
> the discussion at the end. Agenda on board: CSR → stakeholder theory → fostering ethics.

---

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — What CSR Means `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **Defining Corporate Social Responsibility**

> **Deliver:** **CSR** = a company's duty to act for the **benefit of society**, not only its
> shareholders. The crucial word is **duty** — CSR is the claim that a business *owes* something to
> the society that lets it operate (using public infrastructure, workers, resources, and trust).
> Contrast this with the older "the only job of business is to make profit" view, and tell them
> the modern consensus has shifted: profit *and* responsibility.

#### Concept 2 — Carroll's CSR Pyramid `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **Four levels of responsibility**

> 🖼️ Visual: Carroll's pyramid — four stacked layers.

> **Deliver — build it bottom-up, explaining why order matters:**
> 1. **Economic** (base) — *be profitable.* "You can't be responsible if you're bankrupt. Survival funds everything above."
> 2. **Legal** — *obey the law.* The minimum society codifies.
> 3. **Ethical** — *do what's right beyond the law.* The Unit 1 S1 "above the floor" idea.
> 4. **Philanthropic** (top) — *give back* (donations, community programs).
>
> Stress: the lower layers must hold before the upper ones mean anything. A company that donates to
> charity (philanthropic) while underpaying staff (failing ethical) is doing CSR backwards — it's
> buying image without the foundation.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** **Nepal Rastra Bank directs commercial banks to allocate a portion of
  profit to CSR** activities. So in Nepal, part of CSR isn't optional charity — it's **built into
  how the banking sector is regulated.** Telecoms similarly run rural-coverage and education programs.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"CSR is just charity / a PR stunt."*
> ✅ **Correction:** Real CSR is **responsibility embedded in operations** — fair wages, clean
> production, honest marketing, safe products. Donations are only the *visible tip*; the base is
> how you run the business every day.

#### Concept 3 — Stakeholder Theory `[THEORY]` `[~8 min]`

[SLIDE] **Who is a business accountable to?**

> **Deliver:** Introduce the central question: *to whom does a company answer?* The narrow answer
> is **shareholders** (the owners). **Stakeholder theory** widens it: a business answers to
> **everyone materially affected by it** — customers, employees, suppliers, the local community, the
> environment, government, *and* owners. Explain the practical consequence: under a stakeholder
> view, a decision that's great for owners but harmful to the community is a **failure**, not a
> success.

> **Mini case (work it through, ≈3 min):** A factory is **highly profitable** for its owners but
> quietly **pollutes a village's drinking water.** Ask the class to evaluate it twice:
> - *Shareholder lens:* success — profits are up.
> - *Stakeholder lens:* failure — the village (a stakeholder) is harmed, and the cost is just
>   hidden, not absent (health costs, future lawsuits, lost social license to operate).
> Land it: "Ethics fails when we count only the stakeholders who show up on the balance sheet."

#### Concept 4 — Fostering CSR & an Ethical Culture `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **How good ethics gets built (not wished)**

> **Deliver each lever with the *why*:**
> - **Tone from the top** — staff copy what leaders *do*, not what posters *say*. If the CEO cuts an
>   ethical corner, everyone learns corners are fine.
> - **A clear, public code of ethics** — turns vague values into specific expectations.
> - **An ethical culture** — where raising a concern is rewarded, not punished; where "doing the
>   right thing" is the path of least resistance.
> - **Social-responsibility reporting** — publishing your impact creates accountability you can't quietly walk back.

> 🍿 **Fun element:** "Good ethics is good business — and trust **compounds like interest.** Small,
> consistent honest acts build a reputation that pays returns for years; one scandal is a withdrawal
> that can empty the account."

#### 🛠 ACTIVITY — "Genuine or greenwashing?" `[ACTIVITY]` `[~6 min]`

[SLIDE] **Spot the real CSR**

> **Run it (think–pair–share):** Each pair names **one Nepali company's CSR activity** they've seen
> (2 min). Then for 2–3 examples aloud (4 min), the class judges: *genuine responsibility* or
> *image-building*? Give them a test to apply: "Does the company's **everyday operation** match the
> CSR message, or does the ad contradict the behavior?" (e.g. a bank advertising 'we care about
> farmers' while its loan terms crush them = greenwashing).

---

### 🧠 CHECK FOR UNDERSTANDING `[QUIZ]` `[~5 min]`

**MCQ 1.** The **base** of Carroll's CSR pyramid is:
a) philanthropic b) ethical c) legal d) ✅ **economic responsibility**

**MCQ 2.** Stakeholders of a company include all of the following EXCEPT:
a) employees b) customers c) ✅ **only the shareholders** d) the local community

**Discussion prompt:** *Name a Nepali company doing visible CSR — genuine or image-building? Apply the "does daily operation match the message?" test.*

---

### 💡 REAL-LIFE APPLICATION `[~3 min]`
**This matters because…** CSR shapes which companies attract **talent and investment** and which
face **boycotts and bad press**. As an employee you'll help make these choices; as a consumer you
already vote with your money; and as a future manager, knowing the pyramid lets you build CSR that's
real rather than decorative.

---

### 📝 SUMMARY & TAKEAWAYS `[~2 min]`
1. **CSR** = a company's duty to society (Carroll: economic → legal → ethical → philanthropic).
2. **Stakeholders** are everyone affected, not just owners; count the costs that don't show on the balance sheet.
3. Good ethics is **built** through leadership, culture, codes, and transparency.

# S3 — Improving Business Ethics
**Lecture hour 3 of 5 · 50 minutes**

### 🎯 OPENING — Hook `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **"A beautiful Code of Ethics, framed on the wall — and nothing changes. Why?"**

> **Deliver (≈3 min):** Describe it vividly: a company hires consultants, writes an inspiring
> "Code of Ethics," prints it on glossy paper, frames it in reception… and behaves exactly as
> before. Ask the class: **"Why didn't the document change anything?"** Take 2–3 answers.
>
> **Set up (≈2 min):** Pull the answers together: a value written down is not a value *practiced*.
> Ethics needs **systems**, not posters. Today: what those systems actually are, and why some ethics
> programs transform a company while others are expensive wallpaper.

---

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — From Values to Behavior `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **What an ethics program really is**

> **Deliver:** An **ethics program** = the set of **systems** that turn stated values into actual
> behavior. Explain the gap it bridges: every company *says* it values integrity; the program is
> what makes that true on a normal Tuesday under deadline pressure. Give the contrast that proves
> the point: take **two companies with the identical code** — in one, leaders visibly follow it and
> violations have consequences; in the other, leaders quietly break it. **Same document, opposite
> cultures.** The document was never the cause; the system around it was.

#### Concept 2 — Components of an Effective Program `[THEORY]` `[~9 min]`

[SLIDE] **The machinery that works**

> 🖼️ Visual: the ethics-program cycle (Code → Training → Reporting → Audit → Consequences → back to Code).

> **Deliver each component with its purpose:**
> - **A corporate ethics officer / committee** with *real authority* — someone whose job is ethics,
>   not a side duty everyone ignores.
> - **Board-level oversight** — ethics reported to the top, so it can't be buried by middle management.
> - **A practical, specific code of conduct** — not "be good" but "do not accept gifts over X; do not
>   install unlicensed software; report conflicts of interest."
> - **Ongoing ethics training** — real scenarios, refreshed regularly, not a one-time slideshow.
> - **Safe reporting channels (whistle-blower hotlines)** — *anonymous and retaliation-free*, or no
>   one uses them. The whole system fails if people are scared to speak.
> - **Periodic ethics audits** — check that the rules are actually being followed, not just posted.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example (walk it):** a commercial bank's **anti-money-laundering (AML) and compliance
  training**, plus a **grievance/whistle-blower hotline.** This is the concrete machinery that turns
  "we value integrity" into measurable daily practice — and Nepali banks are regulated to have it.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"Having a code of conduct guarantees ethical behavior."*
> ✅ **Correction:** **Enforcement and culture matter far more than the document.** An *unenforced*
> code can be **worse than none** — it signals that the rules are for show, which teaches everyone
> to ignore them.

#### Concept 3 — Measuring & Sustaining Ethics `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **Keeping it real over time**

> **Deliver:** A program isn't "set and forget." Explain the maintenance tools:
> - **Ethics audits** and **anonymous culture surveys** — surface problems before they become scandals.
> - **Consistent consequences** for violations — *at every level*, especially the top.
> - **Tracking** reported concerns and acting on them, so reporting feels worthwhile.
>
> The make-or-break factor is **consistency.** The fastest way to destroy an ethics program is to
> punish violations *selectively.*

> **Mini case (work it, ≈2 min):** Two employees break the **same rule.** The **senior manager is
> quietly let off**; the **junior is publicly punished.** Ask: "What does *everyone watching* now
> believe about this company's ethics?" Draw out: that the code is **power-dependent** — which
> destroys trust *faster than having no code at all*, because now there's visible hypocrisy.

#### 🛠 ACTIVITY — "Design one enforceable rule" `[ACTIVITY]` `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **Make your college exam process more ethical**

> **Run it:** In pairs (2 min), design **one concrete rule** to make the college's exam process more
> ethical — *and* state **how it would be enforced** (a rule with no enforcement is a poster). Take
> 3 answers aloud (3 min) and pressure-test each: "Who checks it? What's the consequence? Could a
> senior person dodge it?" — tying directly back to the consistency point.

---

### 🧠 CHECK FOR UNDERSTANDING `[QUIZ]` `[~5 min]`

**MCQ 1.** The single most important driver of an ethical culture is:
a) a long code document b) ✅ **leadership / tone at the top** c) the company logo d) annual profit

**MCQ 2.** A safe, retaliation-free way for staff to report wrongdoing is a:
a) marketing channel b) ✅ **whistle-blower / reporting hotline** c) sales funnel d) press release

**Discussion prompt:** *Which single component (officer, training, hotline, audit, consequences) would do the most good in a typical Nepali office — and why?*

---

### 💡 REAL-LIFE APPLICATION `[~3 min]`
**This matters because…** in your first weeks at any job, these signals tell you whether you've
joined a **healthy or a toxic** employer: Is there a real reporting channel? Are rules applied to
seniors too? Is training genuine or a checkbox? You'll use this checklist to read a workplace — and
later, to build a good one.

---

### 📝 SUMMARY & TAKEAWAYS `[~2 min]`
1. Values need **systems**, not posters.
2. Effective programs combine **leadership, a specific code, training, safe reporting, and audits.**
3. **Consistent enforcement** beats any document — selective punishment is worse than none.

# S4 — Ethical Considerations in Decision Making
**Lecture hour 4 of 5 · 50 minutes**

### 🎯 OPENING — Hook `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **"The night before the exam, you find leaked answers in a shared drive."**

> **Deliver (≈3 min):** Set the scene in detail: a classmate accidentally shares a Drive folder, and
> inside are **tomorrow's exam answers.** You have them now. Ask the class — *don't* ask "what's
> right" yet, ask **"how would you even decide?"** Take a couple of gut reactions.
>
> **Set up (≈2 min):** Point out that everyone jumped straight to an answer with no *method*. Today
> we build the **method** — a repeatable 5-step process you'll reuse in every later unit (IP,
> security, forensics, cyber law) and every workplace dilemma.

---

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — The 5-Step Decision Process `[THEORY]` `[~9 min]`

[SLIDE] **The spine of the whole course**

> 🖼️ Visual: 5-step flowchart.

> **Deliver — explain each step with what it actually involves:**
> 1. **Get the facts.** Most "ethical dilemmas" dissolve once you have the facts. *Are* these the
>    real answers? Is it actually tomorrow's exam? What don't I know? Never reason on assumptions.
> 2. **Identify stakeholders & options.** Who's affected — you, honest classmates, the teacher, the
>    institution? What are your *realistic* options (not just two extremes)?
> 3. **Evaluate options** using ethical lenses (next concept).
> 4. **Choose and act** — and own it.
> 5. **Reflect** — what was the outcome, and what would you do differently? This is how judgment improves.
>
> Stress: steps 1 and 2 are the ones people skip, and that's where most bad decisions are born.

#### Concept 2 — Four Ethical Lenses `[THEORY]` `[~9 min]`

[SLIDE] **Four ways to test an option**

> **Deliver each lens with a one-line "test question":**
> - **Utilitarian** — *"Which option does the most good for the most people?"* (weighs consequences).
> - **Rights / duty (deontology)** — *"Does this respect everyone's basic rights and my duties, regardless of outcome?"* (some things are wrong even if they'd produce good results).
> - **Fairness / justice** — *"Am I treating similar people alike? Is this fair?"*
> - **Virtue / common good** — *"What would a person of good character do? What serves the community?"*

- 🇳🇵 **Local example (walk it — the converging case):** during a fuel shortage, an **ambulance asks
  to jump the queue.** Test it through every lens:
  - *Utilitarian:* let it pass — a life outweighs everyone's wait.
  - *Rights/duty:* the patient's right to life takes priority.
  - *Fairness:* a medical emergency genuinely *is* a different case, so it isn't unfair.
  - *Virtue:* a decent person waves them through.
  All four **converge** — which is exactly *why* it feels obviously right. Then tell them: the hard
  cases are where lenses **disagree**, and naming the disagreement is half the work.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"Ethics is just personal opinion — anything goes."*
> ✅ **Correction:** Structured reasoning gives **defensible, comparable** answers. Two people using
> the lenses can disagree — but they can *explain and compare* their reasoning. That's the opposite
> of "just opinion."

#### Concept 3 — Common Decision Traps `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **What derails good people**

> **Deliver each trap with how it sounds in real life:**
> - **Rationalization** — *"It's not really cheating, everyone uses these notes."*
> - **Groupthink** — going along because the whole team is, even when it feels wrong.
> - **Slippery slope** — one small compromise ("just this once") that lowers the bar for the next.
> - **Blind obedience to authority** — *"My manager told me to, so it's on them."* (It isn't — you're still responsible.)

> **Mini case (≈2 min):** Your manager says, *"Just fudge this one report — only this once."* Ask
> the class to (a) run it through the 5 steps and (b) **name which trap** is being used to pressure
> them (obedience + slippery slope). This rehearses recognizing pressure in the moment.

#### 🛠 ACTIVITY — "Run the dilemma" `[ACTIVITY]` `[~6 min]`

[SLIDE] **Apply the 5 steps live**

> **Run it:** In pairs (3 min), take the **leaked-exam-answers** hook and work it through **all 5

# S5 — Ethics in Information Technology
**Lecture hour 5 of 5 · 50 minutes · CLOSES UNIT 1**

### 🎯 OPENING — Hook `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **"IT makes ethical mistakes scale to millions — instantly."**

> **Deliver (≈3 min):** Three quick, vivid scenarios:
> - An app **quietly sells your location data** to advertisers.
> - A loan-approval **algorithm rejects applicants by neighborhood** (digital redlining).
> - A **deepfake** of a politician goes viral the night before an election.
>
> Then the framing line: *"A dishonest shopkeeper cheats one customer at a time. A bad line of code
> or a bad data policy can harm **millions in seconds** — and the harm can be **permanent.**"* Ask:
> "What makes the IT versions of these wrongs *worse* than the offline versions?"
>
> **Set up (≈2 min):** That question is the unit's finale — why IT ethics needs its own course, and
> how everything from S1–S4 now applies at machine scale.

---

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — Why IT Raises Distinct Ethical Challenges `[THEORY]` `[~9 min]`

[SLIDE] **What's different about IT**

> **Deliver each factor with a concrete consequence:**
> - **Scale** — one decision (a default setting, an algorithm) affects **millions at once**, not one
>   person at a time.
> - **Speed** — harm spreads in **seconds**, before anyone can intervene or correct it.
> - **Anonymity** — wrongdoers can **hide**; victims can be targeted from a distance, across borders.
> - **Permanence** — data, once leaked or posted, is **almost impossible to fully erase** ("a rumor
>   in a village fades; a screenshot online is forever").
> - **A capability–regulation gap** — technology can do things faster than law and norms catch up, so
>   you often can't rely on "is it legal?" — the law hasn't arrived yet.
>
> Tie back to S1: this is why "the law is the floor" matters *most* in IT — the floor is often
> missing entirely, so your own ethics is all that's holding.

#### Concept 2 — The Four Pillars: "PAPA" `[THEORY]` `[~9 min]`

[SLIDE] **Privacy · Accuracy · Property · Access**

> 🖼️ Visual: PAPA four-quadrant.

> **Deliver — this previews the whole course, so connect each pillar forward:**
> - **Privacy** — who can collect and see your data? → *Unit 2 (privacy), Unit 9 (law).*
> - **Accuracy** — who's responsible when data or an algorithm is **wrong**, and someone is harmed by the error?
> - **Property** — who **owns** information and software? → *Unit 3 (intellectual property).*
> - **Access** — who **gets to use** technology, and who's **left out** (the digital divide)?
>
> Add: **security** threads through all four → *Units 5–8.* So this single slide is a map of the
> entire IT 246 course.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** personal data **leaked from a Nepali online service** (privacy + security);
  **viral misinformation** during elections or after a disaster, spreading panic before facts catch
  up (accuracy). Both are everyday failures happening at IT scale.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"It's just technology — the tool is neutral."*
> ✅ **Correction:** **Technology is not value-neutral.** *What* you choose to build, *what* data you
> collect, and *how* an algorithm decides all **encode human values — and human bias.** A hiring
> algorithm trained on biased data will discriminate, automatically and at scale.

#### Concept 3 — The IT Professional's Responsibility `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **"Can" vs "Should"**

> **Deliver:** The defining question of IT ethics: *just because you **can** build or do something,
> does that mean you **should**?* Explain why this lands on *you*: as a BIM graduate you'll be the
> one choosing what data to collect, how an algorithm treats people, what to log, what to expose.
> Professional **codes (ACM, IEEE)** exist precisely to put your **duty to users and the public**
> above an employer's short-term convenience.

> **Mini case (≈2 min):** For a class project you **can** scrape **100,000 users' public posts** —
> it's technically easy and the data is "public." **Should** you? Run it through the **S4 5-step
> process** quickly as a class: facts (is "public" the same as "consented"?), stakeholders (the
> 100,000 people), lenses (rights/duty: did they agree to *this* use?), act, reflect. Show that
> "can" and "should" give different answers.

#### 🛠 ACTIVITY — "Can vs Should audit" `[ACTIVITY]` `[~6 min]`

[SLIDE] **Map an app you use to PAPA**

> **Run it:** Each pair picks **one app they use daily** (2 min) and identifies (a) one **PAPA**
> theme it raises a concern about, and (b) one "**can but maybe shouldn't**" thing it does with their
> data. Take 3 answers aloud (4 min). This makes the abstract framework personal and closes the unit
> on *their* real digital life.

### 🧠 CHECK FOR UNDERSTANDING `[QUIZ]` `[~5 min]`

**MCQ 1.** Which factor most makes IT ethics *distinct* from everyday ethics?
a) it's cheaper b) ✅ **the scale, speed, and permanence of impact** c) it's always legal d) it needs no rules

**MCQ 2.** In the "PAPA" framework, the four themes are Privacy, Accuracy, Property, and:
a) Advertising b) Authority c) ✅ **Access** d) Automation

**Discussion prompt:** *Name one IT product you use that raises an ethical concern, and explain which PAPA theme it touches and why.*

---

### 💡 REAL-LIFE APPLICATION `[~3 min]`
**This matters because…** as BIM graduates you'll **build, manage, or buy** information systems. *You*
will decide what data to collect, how an algorithm treats people, and what to do when something goes
wrong. Every later unit of this course — IP, cybersecurity, social engineering, forensics, cyber law
— is a specialized tool for the "can vs should" question you met today.

---

### 📝 SUMMARY & TAKEAWAYS `[~2 min]`
1. IT **amplifies** ethical impact through scale, speed, anonymity, and permanence.
2. Technology is **value-laden**, not neutral; the four pillars are **PAPA** (Privacy, Accuracy, Property, Access).
3. Professionals own the **"can vs should"** question — guided by professional codes.

**Next unit (Unit 2):** ethics specifically for **IT workers and IT users** — professional duties, privacy, and social-networking issues.
