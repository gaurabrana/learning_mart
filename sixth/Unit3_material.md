## Unit 3 — Learning Outcomes
By the end of this unit, students will be able to:
1. Define intellectual property and distinguish copyright, patent, trade secret, and trademark.
2. Explain what copyright protects, its duration, and the limits of fair use.
3. Describe what is patentable, the software-patent debate, and the patent process in Nepal.
4. Explain trade secrets, how they are protected, and how they differ from patents.
5. Analyze IP issues — plagiarism, reverse engineering, open source, competitive intelligence, trademark infringement, and cybersquatting — and judge the ethical line in each.

# S11 — Intellectual Property Overview · Copyright
**Lecture hour 1 of 6 (Unit 3) · 50 minutes**

### 🎯 OPENING — Hook `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **"You made it — but do you own it?"**

> **Deliver (≈2 min):** Put three everyday creations on screen and read them slowly:
> 1. You **record a song on your phone.**
> 2. You **write the code** for a class app.
> 3. You **design a logo** for your dai's momo shop.
>
> **Run a quick reaction (≈3 min):** "The moment you create each of these — **who owns them**? And
> what stops a stranger from **selling them as their own?**" Take a few answers. Most students assume
> ownership needs a stamp, a form, a registration. Land the surprise: **the ownership already exists,
> the instant you create it — invisibly.** That's the theme of the whole unit — **intellectual
> property is real property that you can't touch**, and today we map its four families and start with
> the one that protects your song, your code, and your writing: **copyright.**

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Don't resolve ownership fully yet — the point is the *instinct* that "I made it,
> so it's mine" is basically right for copyright, and basically wrong for a name or invention. Write
> the agenda on the board: what is IP → the four families → copyright (what it protects) → duration &
> fair use.

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — Intellectual Property: Property You Can't Touch `[THEORY]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **Four families, one bargain**

> **Deliver:** Start with the definition and write it on the board. **Intellectual property (IP)** =
> **legal rights over creations of the mind — works, inventions, names, and secrets.** Then make the
> central move of the whole unit: there are **four main families**, and each protects a *different
> kind of thing*:
> - **Copyright** protects **expression** (your app's code, a song, a novel).
> - **Patent** protects **inventions** (a new, useful, non-obvious machine or process).
> - **Trade secret** protects **confidential information** kept secret (a recipe, an algorithm).
> - **Trademark** protects **brand identity** (a name, a logo).
>
> Land the *reason IP exists* — the bargain. IP protects **expression and invention, NOT abstract
> ideas**, and it **balances rewarding creators against public access.** Creators get **temporary
> control** so it's worth creating; society **eventually gets access** so knowledge spreads.
> Innovation needs **both** halves — pure monopoly starves the public, pure free-for-all starves the
> creator.

- 🌍 **Global example:** Disney's tight control over **Mickey Mouse** imagery; the **Microsoft
  Windows** codebase — both are creations of the mind worth billions, owned and defended.
- 🇳🇵 **Local example (walk through it):** Nepali IP is administered by the **Department of Industry
  (DoI)** under the **Patent, Design and Trademark Act, 2022 BS (1965)**, while **copyright** sits
  with the **Copyright Registrar's Office.** As a **WTO / TRIPS member**, Nepal must meet
  international IP minimums — so a foreign outsourcing client's IP expectations map onto **real Nepali
  law**, not just goodwill.

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"If it's on the internet, it's free to use."*
> ✅ **Correction — say it out loud:** "**Publication is not permission.** Being able to right-click
> and save a photo, or copy a paragraph, gives you **access, not rights.** Most online content is
> still **owned** by someone. The default is *owned*, not *free.*"

> 🖼️ Visual: the **four families of IP** overview graphic (copyright · patent · trade secret ·
> trademark) — keep it up as the unit's map; you'll return to it every session.

> 🎙️ Speaker note (transition): "Four families, one map. Let's start with the family that already
> protects the song and the code from the hook — copyright."

**📊 Depth table — The four IP families at a glance**

| Family | Protects | Everyday example |
|---|---|---|
| Copyright | Original expression fixed in form | Your app's source code; a song; a novel |
| Patent | A new, useful, non-obvious invention | A drug formula; a novel machine |
| Trade secret | Confidential info kept secret | Wai Wai's masala ratio; a search algorithm |
| Trademark | A brand's identifying sign | The Goldstar name & logo; eSewa's logo |

*ℹ️ Each protects a DIFFERENT thing and is acquired differently — the full master comparison (what/how/how-long) closes the unit in S16.*

#### Concept 2 — Copyright: What It Protects `[THEORY]` `[EXAMPLE]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **Expression, not the idea**

> **Deliver:** Define it precisely. **Copyright** protects **original expression fixed in a tangible
> form** — text, music, film, software code, art. The single most important — and most tested — point:
> it protects the **expression, NOT the underlying idea, fact, or method.** Say it twice.
>
> Explain *why* that line matters: **two people can write different code for the same idea**, and
> each owns their own expression. Facts, ideas, and methods are **free**; only your **particular way
> of expressing them** is protected. Then the mechanism students always get wrong: copyright
> **arises automatically the instant the work is fixed** — **no registration is needed** (though
> registration is powerful *evidence* in a dispute). Your source code and a novel are both
> copyrighted **the instant they're written down.** In Nepal this is governed by the **Copyright
> Act, 2059 (2002)**, registered at the **Copyright Registrar's Office.**

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** piracy of Nepali films and music — a film like ***Kabaddi***, or popular
  songs copied to **YouTube** and sold as **pirated DVDs in New Road** — infringes the creators'
  copyright in their **expression.** The film's *idea* (a rural love-and-marriage comedy) is free for
  anyone; the *specific film* is not.

> **Mini case (pose, take 2–3 answers, ≈2 min):** A student copies a **Nepali band's melody
> note-for-note** into a college jingle and uploads it — **infringement or not?** Draw it out: copying
> the melody note-for-note is taking the **expression**, not being *inspired* by the idea. It's
> **infringement — even for a class project.** The size or motive of the project doesn't change the
> analysis.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"If I change the idea a little, or it's just a class project, copying is
> fine."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Copyright attaches to **expression the instant it's fixed** and covers
> **non-commercial** copying too. Copying a melody note-for-note is taking the expression, not the
> idea — and there's no 'student discount' on infringement."

> 🖼️ Visual: an **idea-vs-expression split** image — the same idea ("split a restaurant bill")
> branching into two entirely different, each-owned implementations.

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Registration is optional but is strong evidence in a dispute. The idea/expression
> split is the single most-tested copyright point — hammer it.

**📊 Depth table — Idea vs expression — what copyright does and doesn't cover**

| The work | The IDEA (not protected) | The EXPRESSION (protected) |
|---|---|---|
| A mobile app | 'An app that splits a restaurant bill' | Your actual code implementing it |
| A song | A chord progression / a genre | The specific recorded melody & lyrics |
| A textbook | The facts and concepts taught | The author's particular wording & layout |
| A photo | 'A sunrise over the Himalayas' | This photographer's specific shot |
| A recipe | The dish and its method | The written wording of the cookbook page |
| A news event | What happened (the facts) | A journalist's specific article about it |

*ℹ️ Anyone may build the same idea; no one may copy your expression of it. This is why two teams can legally build rival bill-splitting apps.*

#### Concept 3 — Copyright Duration & Fair Use `[THEORY]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **It expires — and it has limits**

> **Deliver:** Copyright is **not forever.** It **lasts a limited term, then the work enters the
> public domain** (free for all). In Nepal the general term is the **author's lifetime + 50 years**,
> with defined terms for some categories. The public-domain endpoint is **deliberate** — it's the
> second half of the bargain: society eventually gets **free access to all creative work.**
>
> Then teach the limit that operates *during* the term: **fair use.** Fair use allows **limited use
> for education, research, news, criticism, and parody without permission.** It's a *limited
> exception*, judged by **purpose, the amount used, and the effect on the work's market.** Quoting a
> few lines of a poem for a class essay is fair use; photocopying a **whole textbook** is not. Then
> deliver the correction that saves students the most trouble: **attribution and permission are
> separate.** Crediting the author avoids **plagiarism**; it does **not** grant **copyright
> permission.**

- 🇳🇵 **Local example (walk it — the Bhotahity dilemma):** Quoting a few lines of a **Nepali poem**
  in your essay is **fair use**; **photocopying an entire textbook** to save money at a Bhotahity
  photocopy shop is **infringement** — it copies the whole work and **undercuts the book's market**,
  no matter how unaffordable the original feels. Sympathy for the motive doesn't change the copyright
  math.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"Crediting the author makes copying legal."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Attribution avoids **plagiarism** (an *integrity* offence); it does **not**
> grant copyright **permission** (a *legal* one). You can be a scrupulous citer and **still infringe**
> by copying too much. Two different problems, two different fixes."

> 🍿 **Fun analogy (deliver it):** "Copyright is like **owning a written recipe.** Others may **cook
> the dish** (the idea is free) — but they can't **photocopy and sell your cookbook** (your
> expression). The dish is everyone's; the cookbook page is yours."

**📊 Depth table — Fair use vs infringement — where the line falls (Nepal)**

| Use of the work | Fair use? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Quoting a few lines of a Nepali poem in an essay | Fair use | Small amount, educational, no market harm |
| Photocopying a whole textbook for the class | Infringement | Copies the entire work; harms the book's market |
| A short clip in a news report / review | Fair use | News/criticism purpose, limited amount |
| Uploading a full movie to YouTube | Infringement | Whole work, no permission, destroys its market |
| A parody of a popular song | Often fair use | Parody is a recognised transformative purpose |
| Reposting an artist's full image on your page | Infringement | Whole work copied without permission |

*ℹ️ Fair use turns on purpose, how much you take, and market effect — not on whether you credited the author. Attribution avoids plagiarism, not infringement.*

#### 🛠 ACTIVITY — "Fair use or infringement?" `[ACTIVITY]` `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **Decide three acts you've actually done or seen**

> **Run it:** In pairs (2 min), students decide **fair use or infringement** for three acts they've
> actually done or seen. Seeds: **sharing a full PDF textbook** in a class group; **quoting two lines
> of a song** in a caption; **reposting a meme.** Take **3–4 answers aloud** (3 min) and test each
> with the three factors: **purpose + amount + market effect.** Close: "Notice that **attribution
> didn't decide any of them** — that's the key trap."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Push students past "but I credited it" — crediting avoids plagiarism, not
> infringement. The full-textbook PDF is the clearest infringement (whole work, market harm); a
> two-line quote is the clearest fair use.

### 💡 REAL-LIFE APPLICATION `[~3 min]`
**This matters because…** every **project, blog, song, or app you publish** is **automatically
copyrighted** — and so is **everything you might be tempted to copy.** Knowing the **idea/expression
line** protects you from *both* directions: it stops you infringing others by copying too much, and
it tells you what you can legitimately defend as your own. In a job, "just grab it off the internet"
is one of the fastest ways to expose your employer to a legal claim — the habit you build today
travels with you.

### 📝 SUMMARY & TAKEAWAYS `[~2 min]`
1. **IP = legal rights over creations of the mind**, in **four families** (copyright, patent, trade secret, trademark).
2. **Copyright protects fixed expression automatically** — the **expression, not the idea** — under Nepal's **Copyright Act 2059**.
3. Copyright **expires** (life + 50 yrs → public domain) and has **fair-use limits**; **attribution ≠ permission**.

# S12 — Patent
**Lecture hour 2 of 6 (Unit 3) · 50 minutes**

### 🎯 OPENING — Hook `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **"Could one company have patented the mobile wallet — and shut the other down?"**

> **Deliver (≈2 min):** Make it concrete and local. "**Khalti and eSewa** both let you pay by phone.
> Imagine one of them had **patented 'paying by mobile wallet'** first. Could it have **legally shut
> the other down**?"
>
> **Run the question (≈3 min):** "Why do **drug prices** and **phone features** hinge on this one
> question?" Take a few answers. Land it: a **patent grants a temporary monopoly** on an invention —
> a much stronger, rarer right than copyright. It's a deliberate **trade**: you get a monopoly now, in
> exchange for **publicly disclosing** how your invention works, so society learns from it and
> eventually gets it for free. Today: **what's patentable**, the **software-patent debate**, and the
> **patent process.**

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Keep the eSewa/Khalti question open as a thread — it exposes both the *power* of
> patents and *why software patents are contested* (Concept 2). Agenda on board: what's patentable →
> the software-patent debate → the patent process.

---

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — Patent: What It Is and What Is Patentable `[THEORY]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **A monopoly in exchange for disclosure**

> **Deliver:** A **patent** = an **exclusive right granted for an invention** — a **new, useful,
> non-obvious product or process** — **in exchange for public disclosure.** Contrast it with S11:
> copyright protects the **expression**; a patent protects the **functional idea itself.** It
> typically lasts about **20 years**, then the invention becomes **public.**
>
> Then teach the gate — the **three patentability tests** an invention must pass:
> - **Novelty** — is it **genuinely new** (not already known or published)?
> - **Inventiveness (non-obviousness)** — is it **non-obvious to a skilled expert**?
> - **Industrial applicability** — is it **useful and can it be made**?
>
> Stress the disclosure bargain: you must **publicly disclose how it works** — that's the *public's*
> payoff for granting you the monopoly.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** patents are registered with the **Department of Industry, Nepal** under the
  Patent, Design and Trademark Act. In practice Nepal has **relatively few local tech patents** —
  most filings are **designs, agricultural, or herbal products** — which is exactly why a Nepali
  software team rarely relies on patents at all (Concept 2).

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"You can patent any good idea."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "**Pure ideas, discoveries, and laws of nature are NOT patentable** — only a
> **concrete, novel, non-obvious, useful invention** is. 'I thought of mobile payments first' patents
> nothing; a **specific new mechanism** for doing it might."

> 🖼️ Visual: the patentability **3-test funnel** — novel → non-obvious → useful; an invention drops
> through only if it passes all three.

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Contrast with S11: copyright would protect the *code* of a wallet app; a patent
> would protect the *method* — a much stronger, rarer right. That contrast sets up Concept 2.

**📊 Depth table — The three patentability tests**

| Test | The question it asks | Fails if… |
|---|---|---|
| Novelty | Is it genuinely new? | It's already known or published (prior art exists) |
| Inventiveness (non-obviousness) | Is it non-obvious to a skilled expert? | An expert would find it an obvious next step |
| Industrial applicability | Is it useful and can it be made? | It's abstract, a pure discovery, or a law of nature |

*ℹ️ All three must pass. 'Being popular' or 'being clever' is not enough — and pure ideas/discoveries fail the third test outright.*

#### Concept 2 — Software Patents: The Debate `[THEORY]` `[EXAMPLE]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **Copyright covers the code; a patent would cover the method**

> **Deliver:** **Software patents** cover **software-implemented methods or algorithms** — and they
> are **controversial worldwide.** Set up the key distinction: **copyright already covers the CODE**;
> a **patent would cover the METHOD / function** itself. That's a much bigger hammer — a broad
> software patent can **block a whole approach**, not just one product.
>
> Explain *why* it's contested. Many jurisdictions — **and Nepal in practice** — **restrict or
> disallow pure-software patents**, partly because of the **"patent troll"** problem: entities that
> **acquire patents only to sue, not to build.** So the realistic answer for most software isn't
> "patent it" — it's **copyright + trade secret**, sometimes open source.

- 🌍 **Global example:** the **Amazon "1-Click"** patent and the **Apple vs Samsung** smartphone
  patent wars show how far software/design patents can reach — and how much litigation they generate.
- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** a Nepali **fintech innovation** is usually protected by **copyright + trade
  secret, not patent**, given local limits — the code is copyrighted automatically and the clever
  bits are kept secret.

> **Mini case (work it, ≈2 min):** A **Kathmandu startup invents a clever load-balancing
> algorithm.** Three options: **patent it** (public disclosure, ~20-yr monopoly — but pure-software
> patents are largely **unavailable in Nepal** and costly), **keep it a trade secret** (private,
> unlimited — but **no protection** if a rival independently invents it), or **open-source it**
> (community adoption, no monopoly). Draw out that **most local teams choose copyright + trade
> secret** — and that's the realistic professional answer.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"We should patent our software to protect it."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "For pure software this is often **impossible or impractical** — copyright
> already protects the code, and software patents are **restricted (including in Nepal) and
> expensive.** The realistic tools are **copyright + trade secret**, sometimes open source."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Keep the Nepal-startup angle central. The takeaway isn't "patents are bad" — it's
> "for pure software in Nepal, patent is usually the *weakest real option.*"

**📊 Depth table — Copyright vs patent — for the same piece of software**

| Question | Copyright | Patent |
|---|---|---|
| What does it cover? | The specific written code | The underlying method / function |
| How strong is it? | Blocks copying your code | Blocks anyone using the technique at all |
| How do you get it? | Automatic on writing the code | Apply, disclose, pass 3 tests, pay fees |
| Available for pure software? | Yes — code is copyrightable | Often restricted / disallowed (incl. Nepal in practice) |
| Typical use by a Nepali startup | Yes — protects their codebase | Rare — copyright + trade secret used instead |

*ℹ️ This is why 'just copyright it' is usually the realistic answer for Nepali software — patents on pure software are contested and largely unavailable locally.*

#### Concept 3 — The Patent Process `[THEORY]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **Slow, costly, public, and territorial**

> **Deliver:** Getting a patent is a **defined sequence**: **prior-art search → file an application
> with claims → examination → grant → maintenance/renewal fees.** The **claims** precisely mark the
> **boundaries** of what you're monopolising — like a land survey.
>
> Then name the **four traits** the system demands, because they drive every real-world patent
> decision: it is **slow** (examination takes years), **costly** (filing, attorney, renewal fees),
> **public** (your invention is fully disclosed — competitors can read exactly how it works), and
> **territorial** (a **Nepal patent gives no protection in India or the US**). Global protection means
> filing — and paying — in **each country separately.** Cost and effort push many Nepali startups to
> **file abroad or skip patents entirely.**

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** a Nepali startup weighing a patent files at the **DoI**, but faces the
  **cost/effort of a slow, public, territorial process** — and a Nepal patent **won't protect them
  in their export markets.** That calculus pushes many to file abroad (expensive) or skip patents and
  rely on **trade secrets and speed-to-market** instead.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"Once I have a patent, I'm protected everywhere."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Patents are **territorial** — a Nepal patent means **nothing in India, the US,
> or the EU.** Worldwide protection means filing and paying in **each jurisdiction separately**, which
> is why global patents are a **big-company game.**"

> 🍿 **Fun analogy (deliver it):** "A patent is like **staking a land claim.** You must **publicly
> mark your boundaries** (the claims) so everyone can see exactly what's yours — and after the
> **lease (20 years)**, the land **becomes public** again."

> 🖼️ Visual: the patent-process **timeline** — search → file claims → examine → grant → renew
> (~20 yrs), one country at a time.

> 🎙️ Speaker note: The land-claim analogy ties disclosure and territoriality together — you mark
> boundaries publicly, and the lease ends. This explains why patents suit visible, high-value
> inventions and why software teams prefer trade secrets (S13).

**📊 Depth table — Four things the patent system demands**

| Trait | What it means for you | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Slow | Examination can take years | Not a quick fix for a fast-moving product |
| Costly | Filing, attorney, and renewal fees | Prices out many small Nepali startups |
| Public | Your invention is fully disclosed | Rivals learn exactly how it works |
| Territorial | A Nepal patent protects only in Nepal | Global cover needs a filing per country |

*ℹ️ These four traits together explain why patents suit visible, high-value, reverse-engineerable inventions — and why software teams often prefer trade secrets (S13).*

#### 🛠 ACTIVITY — "Patent, secret, or open?" `[ACTIVITY]` `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **Recommend a protection strategy**

> **Run it:** In pairs (2 min), a **Kathmandu startup has a clever new algorithm.** Students
> recommend **patent / trade secret / open source**, stating **one reason for and one against** their
> choice. Take **3 answers aloud** (3 min) and surface the trade-offs: **cost, disclosure,
> reverse-engineerability, community adoption.** Close: "For **pure software in Nepal**, patent is
> usually the **weakest real option.**"

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Good answers weigh — is it reverse-engineerable once shipped? Is it costly to
> patent? Is community adoption valuable? Most land on **copyright + trade secret**, occasionally open
> source for adoption.

# S13 — Trade Secrets
**Lecture hour 3 of 6 (Unit 3) · 50 minutes**

### 🎯 OPENING — Hook `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **"How is *silence* a legal strategy?"**

> **Deliver (≈2 min):** Name three famous things and reveal the twist. "**Wai Wai's exact masala
> blend. KFC's spice mix. Google's search ranking.** What do they have in common? **None of them is
> patented.**"
>
> **Run the question (≈3 min):** "So how are they protected?" Take a few guesses, then land it:
> **they're protected by keeping quiet.** These could all be patented — but that would **disclose
> them and put a 20-year clock on them.** Instead their owners chose the opposite: **secrecy that can
> last forever.** That's the **trade secret** — the patent's silent rival. Today: **what a trade
> secret is → how it's protected → trade secret vs patent.**

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Draw out that these could be patented but *deliberately aren't* — because a patent
> expires and discloses, while a secret can last forever. That contrast is the spine of the whole
> session. Agenda on board.

---

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — Trade Secret: Definition `[THEORY]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **Forever — until someone else figures it out**

> **Deliver:** A **trade secret** = **confidential business information that gives a competitive edge
> and is kept secret through reasonable effort.** It covers **formulas, recipes, processes, customer
> lists, algorithms, and source code.** Two defining features make it strange and powerful:
> - **No government grant and no filing** — the protection **IS the secrecy** plus reasonable effort.
> - It can last **indefinitely** — potentially **forever** (Coca-Cola's formula is over a century old
>   and still secret).
>
> Then deliver the catch that keeps it honest: a trade secret offers **NO protection against
> independent discovery or legitimate reverse engineering.** The moment the secret **leaks or is
> figured out**, the protection **evaporates.** It is unlimited **but fragile.**

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** a **noodle/masala brand's spice ratio (Wai Wai / CG Foods)**, a
  **restaurant's signature recipe**, or a **remittance company's pricing model** are classic trade
  secrets — valuable, kept confidential, and protected **simply by not telling anyone.** No form is
  filed; the **secrecy itself is the protection.**

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"A trade secret is basically the same as a patent."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "**Opposite, in fact.** A patent is **public and time-limited**; a trade secret
> is **private and unlimited** — but a patent **protects you even if a rival independently invents**
> the same thing, while a trade secret gives you **nothing** against independent discovery or
> legitimate reverse engineering."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: The contrast that matters: **patent = public + time-limited; trade secret =
> private + unlimited but fragile.** This table previews the S16 master comparison.

**📊 Depth table — Trade secret vs patent — the key confusable**

| Dimension | Trade secret | Patent |
|---|---|---|
| Public or private? | Private — kept confidential | Public — fully disclosed |
| How long? | Unlimited — while it stays secret | ~20 years, then public domain |
| Registration? | None | Formal application & fees |
| Protects against independent discovery? | No — rival may invent it too | Yes — monopoly even vs independent inventors |
| Protects against reverse engineering? | No — legal reverse engineering breaks it | Yes — within the patent term |

*ℹ️ Same invention, opposite strategies: a patent trades secrecy for a strong-but-temporary monopoly; a trade secret trades that monopoly for potentially-forever secrecy — but with no protection if independently found.*

#### Concept 2 — How Trade Secrets Are Protected `[THEORY]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **You only own it if you tried to keep it**

> **Deliver:** Trade secrets are protected by **secrecy MEASURES, not a government grant.** Walk the
> toolkit and *why each matters*:
> - **NDAs (non-disclosure agreements)** — legally **bind people who must know** the secret to keep it.
> - **Confidentiality clauses** in employment contracts — the same duty, built into the job.
> - **Access controls + "need-to-know"** — **shrink the circle** of people who could leak it.
> - **Non-compete agreements** — limit a departing employee from **directly helping a rival.**
>
> Then the legal hinge: the law gives a **remedy only if the secret was MISAPPROPRIATED** —
> **stolen**, not independently discovered. And a critical requirement: **"reasonable effort"
> matters legally** — a secret you **didn't try to protect** may **not count as one.** If a rival
> **independently invents** or **legally reverse-engineers** it, that's **not misappropriation** — no
> remedy.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** **NDAs in Nepali IT outsourcing / BPO firms** are the everyday face of
  this — you'll sign one on day one. The classic tension: the **worry when a developer leaves for a
  competitor** carrying knowledge of the code in their head.

> **Mini case (work it, ≈2 min):** A **developer leaves a Lalitpur software house and rebuilds a
> similar module from memory** at a rival. **Theft of a secret, or fair reuse of skill?** Draw the
> line: **general skills and know-how** you carry in your head are **yours to reuse**; the employer's
> **specific confidential code, data, or documented processes** are **not.** NDAs and non-competes
> are what make that line **enforceable.**

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"If I didn't copy any files, I can't have stolen a trade secret."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Misappropriation covers **taking confidential information, not just files** —
> rebuilding a **protected proprietary process from memory** can still breach an NDA. But **genuinely
> general skill** you learned is yours; the hard cases sit **exactly on that boundary.**"

> 🎙️ Speaker note: "Reasonable effort" is a legal requirement — a company that didn't lock down its
> secret may lose the right to call it one. Independent discovery is *never* misappropriation.

**📊 Depth table — How a trade secret is actually protected**

| Protection measure | What it does | Nepal / IT example |
|---|---|---|
| NDA / confidentiality clause | Legally binds insiders to keep quiet | Standard in Nepali IT/BPO employment contracts |
| Access controls | Limits who can even see the secret | Source code repo restricted to a small team |
| Need-to-know | Shares the secret only where essential | Only two staff know the full masala ratio |
| Non-compete agreement | Limits leaving to help a direct rival | A departing developer's contract terms |
| Legal remedy (misappropriation) | Sue only if the secret was stolen | No remedy if a rival independently invents it |

*ℹ️ 'Reasonable effort' is a legal requirement — a company that didn't lock down its secret may lose the right to call it one. Independent discovery is never misappropriation.*

#### Concept 3 — Trade Secret vs Patent: Choosing `[THEORY]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **Which weapon for which asset?**

> **Deliver:** Choosing how to protect an invention is a **strategic call**, not a "stronger is
> better" reflex. Give the **rule of thumb**:
> - Use a **TRADE SECRET** if it's **hard to reverse-engineer** and its value is **long-term**
>   (recipes, internal/server-side algorithms).
> - Use a **PATENT** if it will be **sold or visible** and is **reverse-engineerable** (a machine, a
>   device mechanism).
>
> Explain the logic: if rivals can **take your product apart and copy it**, secrecy **won't hold** —
> patent it. If the secret can be **kept behind closed doors**, a trade secret can **outlast any
> patent.** And note the pro move: **many products use BOTH** — patent the **visible mechanism**,
> keep the **tuning/parameters secret.**

- 🇳🇵 **Local example (run it):** **Wai Wai keeps its masala ratio a trade secret** rather than
  patenting it — the recipe is **hard to reverse-engineer** from the finished noodle, and a patent
  would both **disclose it publicly** and **expire in ~20 years.** Secrecy gives **potentially
  unlimited** protection — exactly right for a recipe that **never ships in a copyable form.**

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"Stronger protection is always better, so always patent."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Not so — patenting a recipe would **publish it and put a 20-year clock on it**,
> when keeping it secret could protect it **forever.** The right choice depends on
> **reverse-engineerability and time horizon**, not on which right *sounds* strongest."

> 🍿 **Fun analogy (deliver it):** "A patent is **shouting your invention from the rooftop in exchange
> for 20 years of rent.** A trade secret is **whispering it and hoping no one overhears — forever.**"

> 🖼️ Visual: a **"Patent vs Trade Secret" decision tree** — reverse-engineerable & visible → patent;
> hard to copy & long-term value → secret.

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Run the Wai Wai example as the anchor — keep the recipe secret (hard to
> reverse-engineer, forever value) rather than patent it (which would disclose it and expire). The
> deciding question is always "once it's in customers' hands, can they figure it out?"

**📊 Depth table — Which protection fits which asset?**

| The asset | Best protection | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Coca-Cola's formula | Trade secret | Hard to reverse-engineer; value is long-term |
| A new drug molecule | Patent | Reverse-engineerable once sold; needs strong monopoly |
| Google's search ranking | Trade secret | Runs server-side; never shipped to be copied |
| A novel machine mechanism | Patent | Visible in the product; rivals could copy it |
| Wai Wai's masala ratio | Trade secret | Kept in-house; would only expire if patented |
| A consumer gadget's circuit | Patent | Ships to customers who can open it up |

*ℹ️ The deciding question: once it's in customers' hands, can they figure it out? If yes → patent. If it stays behind your walls → trade secret.*

> Today: **plagiarism → reverse engineering.**

> 🎙️ Speaker note: The key mental model for the whole session — **'wrong' (ethics) and 'illegal'
> (law) are separate axes.** An act can be one, both, or neither. Agenda on board: plagiarism →

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — Plagiarism `[THEORY]` `[~15 min]`

[SLIDE] **An integrity offence — separate from the law**

> **Deliver:** **Plagiarism** = **presenting someone else's work or ideas as your own, without
> credit.** The critical framing: it is an **ETHICAL / academic offence** — **distinct from copyright
> infringement, which is a LEGAL one** — though they **often overlap.** Draw the two axes on the
> board:
> - **Plagiarism can occur even with uncopyrighted material** (copying an idea with no credit).
> - **Infringement can occur even when you credit the source** (copying too much protected
>   expression).
>
> Then widen the definition to what students actually do today: it includes **self-plagiarism**
> (reusing your own past submitted work as new) and **passing off AI-generated text as your own
> original work.** And the trap: **paraphrasing without real understanding, or copying structure, is
> still plagiarism.** Citing avoids plagiarism **only if** the work is **genuinely attributed AND
> quoted properly** — a reference dumped at the end does **not launder copied text.**

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** **copy-paste BIM project reports and thesis sections** lifted from **seniors
  or online** are plagiarism under **Tribhuvan University's** rules — an integrity offence that can
  **fail you regardless of whether the source was copyrighted.** **AI-generated essays** submitted as
  your own are the **same offence in a new form** — the fastest-growing kind today.

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"Changing a few words, or citing the source, means it's not
> plagiarism."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Paraphrasing **without real understanding** — or keeping the original's
> **structure and ideas** — is **still plagiarism**, and a citation only helps if the work is
> **genuinely attributed and properly quoted.** Sprinkling a reference at the end does **not launder**
> copied text."

> 🍿 **Fun analogy (deliver it):** "Plagiarism is **wearing someone else's medal in your own race
> photo.** You didn't run the race — you just borrowed the proof that you did."

> 🖼️ Visual: a **"plagiarism vs copyright infringement" Venn** — two circles that overlap in the
> middle but each extend into their own zone (ethics-only vs law-only).

> 🎙️ Speaker note: TU's plagiarism rules are real and enforced on reports and theses. Note row 2 vs
> row 6 in the table below — citing helps ONLY if the wording is genuinely your own or properly
> quoted.

**📊 Depth table — Is it plagiarism? — common student cases**

| What you did | Plagiarism? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Copy-paste a senior's project report as your own | Yes | Presenting others' work as yours, no credit |
| Paraphrase a source but cite it properly | No | Genuine attribution; your own wording |
| Submit an AI-generated essay as your own | Yes | Passing off non-original work as yours |
| Reuse your OWN past submitted essay as new | Yes (self-plagiarism) | Presenting old work as new original work |
| Quote two lines and attribute them | No | Properly quoted and credited |
| Reword a paragraph but keep its structure/ideas, no credit | Yes | Copied structure/ideas without attribution |

*ℹ️ Tribhuvan University enforces anti-plagiarism rules on reports and theses. Note row 2 vs row 6: citing helps ONLY if the wording is genuinely your own or properly quoted.*

#### Concept 2 — Reverse Engineering `[THEORY]` `[EXAMPLE]` `[~15 min]`

[SLIDE] **Understand or interoperate — not copy**

> **Deliver:** **Reverse engineering** = **analysing a finished product to discover how it was built
> or how it works.** Deliver the surprising-to-students truth first: it is **legal and common** for
> three legitimate purposes — **interoperability** (making products work together), **security
> research** (finding vulnerabilities, studying malware), and **learning** (understanding is not
> copying). Whole **security careers** depend on it.
>
> Then draw the line: it **crosses into misuse** when used to **copy protected code** or
> **misappropriate a trade secret**, and **EULAs often forbid it outright** — adding a **contract**
> issue on top. Teach the lawful workaround: a **"clean-room" re-implementation** — rebuild from
> **behaviour, not from the code** — is the legitimate way to match a function. The deciding factors
> are **intent and use**, not the technique itself.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example (same technique, opposite ethics):** a Nepali developer **decompiles eSewa's
  app** — to build a **compatible tool** that works alongside it (**legitimate interoperability**)
  versus to **clone its features and UI 1:1** (**copying protected expression**). **Same technique,
  opposite ethics** — the purpose and what you do with the result decide which side of the line you're
  on.

> **Mini case (pose, take 2–3 answers, ≈2 min):** A team **reverse-engineers eSewa's app to build a
> *compatible* tool** vs **to clone its features.** Which is ethical? Draw out: **interoperability =
> legitimate**; **1:1 cloning of protected code = misuse.** The *act* of decompiling is identical —
> the **purpose and the output** are what differ.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"Reverse engineering is hacking / always illegal."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "It's a **standard, lawful practice** for interoperability, security research,
> and learning — **whole security careers depend on it.** It becomes wrong **only** when used to copy
> protected code, steal a trade secret, or breach a EULA that forbids it."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Security careers depend on **ethical** reverse engineering (studying malware).
> Intent and use define the line — keep returning to that phrase. Note the EULA row adds a *contract*
> issue distinct from copyright.

**📊 Depth table — Reverse engineering — legitimate use vs misuse**

| Purpose | Legitimate? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Building a tool that interoperates with an app | Legitimate | Interoperability is a recognised lawful purpose |
| Security research (studying malware, finding bugs) | Legitimate | Protects users; standard security practice |
| Learning how a technique works | Legitimate | Understanding is not copying |
| Cloning an app's protected code 1:1 | Misuse | Copies protected expression — infringement |
| Extracting a competitor's trade secret | Misuse | Misappropriation of a trade secret |
| Reverse-engineering where the EULA forbids it | Misuse (contract) | Breaches the licence you agreed to |

*ℹ️ Intent and use draw the line: understanding/interoperability/security = legitimate; copying protected code or breaking a secret/contract = misuse.*

### 🎯 OPENING — Hook `[~5 min]`

[SLIDE] **"Free to copy — yet worth billions. How?"**

> **Deliver (≈2 min):** Open with the paradox. "**Linux, Android, and most of the internet** run on
> code **anyone can copy for free** — yet **companies make billions** on it. And firms **legally
> 'spy' on competitors** every single day."
>
> **Run the question (≈3 min):** "**How is any of this allowed?**" Take a few guesses. Land the two
> answers this session gives: **licensing** (open source isn't 'no rules' — it's copyright *plus* a
> licence) and the **ethics of intelligence-gathering** (there's a legal, public way to study rivals —
> and an illegal, dishonest way). Today: **open source → competitive intelligence vs espionage.**

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Bust the myth up front that "open source = no rules." Agenda on board: open source
> (copyright + a licence) → competitive intelligence vs espionage.

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — Open Source Code `[THEORY]` `[~15 min]`

[SLIDE] **Copyright PLUS a licence — not the absence of it**

> **Deliver:** **Open source software** is **released under a licence that grants rights to use,
> study, modify, and share the source.** Deliver the single most important correction immediately:
> **open source is NOT the absence of copyright — it's copyright PLUS a licence.** The author **keeps
> copyright** and uses the licence to **grant specific rights on specific conditions.**
>
> Then split the two families:
> - **Permissive** (**MIT, Apache**) — use it **almost anywhere**, including in closed software; the
>   main obligation is just to **keep the attribution / licence notice.**
> - **Copyleft** (**GPL**) — if you **distribute a derivative**, you **must release its source under
>   the same terms.** This is the "share-alike" rule.
>
> Land the consequence: **violating the licence** — e.g. **shipping GPL code inside a closed
> product**, or **dropping attribution** — is **copyright infringement.** Companies have been **sued**
> for exactly this.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** **Nepali startups and government projects** build on **WordPress, Linux, and
  open frameworks to cut cost** — which is exactly what open source is *for.* But the licences carry
  **obligations**: keep **attribution** for MIT/Apache code, and **open your source** if you
  distribute a **GPL-based derivative.** Honouring the licence is **not optional politeness — it's the
  condition of the grant.**

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"Open source means free with no rules — I can use it however I like."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Open source is **copyright plus a licence**, and the licence imposes **real
> obligations** (attribution, share-alike). **Ignoring them is infringement** — companies have been
> **sued** for shipping GPL code inside closed products."

> 🖼️ Visual: a **licence spectrum bar** — Public Domain → MIT/Apache (permissive) → GPL (copyleft) →
> Proprietary — with obligations increasing as you move right through the open licences.

> 🎙️ Speaker note: The "free = no rules" myth is the key correction. Both permissive and copyleft are
> *real copyright licences with enforceable obligations* — "open" means "licensed on conditions," not
> "do whatever you like."

**📊 Depth table — Permissive vs copyleft — the two open-source families**

| Question | Permissive (MIT, Apache) | Copyleft (GPL) |
|---|---|---|
| Can you use it in closed software? | Yes | Only if you also open your derivative |
| Main obligation | Keep the attribution / licence notice | Release derivative source under the same licence |
| Why a company might avoid it | (rarely avoided) | Can 'infect' proprietary code with openness |
| Example projects | React (MIT); much of Apache's stack | Linux, Android core (GPL) |
| Violation = | Dropping attribution → infringement | Shipping GPL code closed → infringement |

*ℹ️ Both are real copyright licences with enforceable obligations — 'open' means 'licensed on conditions', not 'do whatever you like'.*

#### Concept 2 — Competitive Intelligence: Ethical vs Espionage `[THEORY]` `[EXAMPLE]` `[~15 min]`

[SLIDE] **The method defines the line**

> **Deliver:** **Competitive intelligence (CI)** = **legally and ethically gathering PUBLIC
> information about competitors to inform strategy.** Normalize it first: CI is **legal business
> homework** — reading what a rival has **already made public.** It uses **public, legal sources** —
> **websites, ads, public filings, job posts, trade shows.**
>
> Then draw the sharp line: CI becomes **industrial ESPIONAGE** when it involves **theft, bribery,
> hacking, misrepresentation, or stealing trade secrets.** Deliver the key insight: **the line is
> crossed by HOW you get the information, not by wanting it.** Espionage means **hacking systems,
> bribing staff, posing as someone else** (misrepresentation is a classic tactic), or **lifting trade
> secrets.** Wanting the information is fine; **stealing or lying to get it is not.**

- 🇳🇵 **Local example (walk the spectrum):** a Nepali **bank studying competitors' published interest
  rates and branch networks** is doing **legitimate CI.** The **same bank bribing a rival's staffer
  for a customer list**, or **breaching their systems**, is **industrial espionage — a crime.** And a
  **salesperson posing as a customer** to extract a rival's **secret pricing** is **espionage by
  misrepresentation**, even though **no system was hacked.**

> **Mini case (pose, take 2–3 answers, ≈2 min):** A **salesperson poses as a customer to extract a
> rival's secret pricing** — **CI or espionage?** Draw it out: it's **espionage** — the method is
> **misrepresentation (lying about who you are)**, even though nothing was "hacked." The **dishonest
> method** is what crosses the line.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"If I can get the information, gathering it is fine."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "What matters is **HOW you obtain it.** Public, honest sources are **fair game**;
> **theft, bribery, hacking, or lying** about who you are turn research into **espionage — illegal and
> unethical — regardless of how useful the information is.**"

> 🍿 **Fun analogy (deliver it):** "**Competitive intelligence** is **reading the other team's
> published playbook.** **Espionage** is **breaking into their locker room to steal it.** Same
> information — completely different act."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Analysing a rival's public annual report is CI; hacking their servers is
> espionage. The salesperson-posing-as-a-customer case is the sharp edge — misrepresentation is
> espionage even with no hacking.

**📊 Depth table — Competitive intelligence vs industrial espionage**

| Method | CI or espionage? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Reading a rival's public annual report / rates | CI | Public, legal source |
| Studying competitors' ads and job postings | CI | Freely published information |
| Walking a competitor's public trade-show booth | CI | Openly available to anyone |
| Hacking a rival's servers for their data | Espionage | Illegal intrusion / theft |
| Bribing a staffer for a customer list | Espionage | Theft of a trade secret via bribery |
| Posing as a customer to extract secret pricing | Espionage | Misrepresentation to obtain confidential info |

*ℹ️ The test is HOW you obtain it: public + honest = CI; theft, bribery, hacking, or lies = espionage (illegal and unethical).*

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — Trademark & Trademark Infringement `[THEORY]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **Protecting the source — and the customer from confusion**

> **Deliver:** A **trademark** is a **sign, name, logo, or slogan that identifies the SOURCE of goods
> or services.** **Infringement** is **unauthorized use of a confusingly similar mark.** Deliver the
> core purpose: trademarks **protect brand identity and prevent consumer confusion** — the harm is
> that **buyers can't tell who really made the product.**
>
> Then three features students routinely get wrong:
> - A mark can last **indefinitely** — **as long as it's used and renewed** — unlike copyright and
>   patents which expire.
> - Infringement **doesn't need an identical copy** — a **confusingly SIMILAR** mark is enough.
> - **Company registration and trademark registration are SEPARATE filings.**
>
> In Nepal, trademarks are **registered at the Department of Industry** under the Patent, Design and
> Trademark Act.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** **fake "Goldstar" shoes**, **counterfeit "Wai Wai" packets**, and **copycat
  momo/restaurant logos** around Kathmandu are **trademark infringement**: they use a **confusingly
  similar mark** so buyers **think they're getting the genuine brand.** Well-known trademark disputes
  are **filed at the DoI.**

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"Registering my company name means I own the trademark."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "**Company registration and trademark registration are separate filings** —
> registering a company **doesn't secure the brand mark**, and vice versa. To protect a brand identity
> you must **register the trademark specifically.**"

> 🎙️ Speaker note: The counterfeit Goldstar / Wai Wai cases make it concrete — the **confusion**
> (buyers think it's genuine) *is* the harm. The test is **consumer confusion about SOURCE.**

**📊 Depth table — Trademark infringement — what crosses the line**

| Situation | Infringement? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Selling fake 'Goldstar' shoes with the logo | Yes | Uses the mark to pass off fakes as genuine |
| Counterfeit 'Wai Wai' packets | Yes | Confuses buyers about the true source |
| A momo stall using a near-identical famous logo | Yes | Confusingly similar mark → consumer confusion |
| Your own original shop name & logo | No | Distinct mark; no confusion with others |
| Naming a product with a common dictionary word | Usually no | Generic terms are weak/unprotectable marks |
| Comparative ad naming a rival truthfully | Usually no | Nominative reference, not passing off |

*ℹ️ The test is consumer confusion about SOURCE. Registered TM disputes in Nepal are filed at the Department of Industry.*

#### Concept 2 — Cybersquatting `[THEORY]` `[EXAMPLE]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **Grabbing a brand's domain in bad faith**

> **Deliver:** **Cybersquatting** = **registering a domain name matching someone else's trademark in
> bad faith** — to **resell it or divert traffic.** Deliver the defining element: it's the **BAD
> FAITH** — grabbing a **known brand's name to profit from it** — that makes it cybersquatting.
>
> Then draw the boundaries carefully so students don't over-apply it:
> - **Legitimate domain investing** (generic names, **no target brand**) is **different and lawful.**
> - **Typosquatting** registers **misspellings** (e-sewa vs esewa) to catch **mistyped traffic** —
>   often for **phishing.** It's a **close cousin.**
> - **Disputes:** **`.com` via the UDRP** (ICANN's dispute process); **`.np` domains** are managed by
>   **Mercantile (register.com.np)**, which handles local disputes.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example (work it):** a person **registers a `.com.np` domain matching a famous Nepali
  brand** and offers to **sell it back for Rs 5 lakh** — that's **cybersquatting** (bad-faith grab to
  ransom the name), **not** legitimate business, and the brand can challenge it through the **`.np`
  registry.** A lookalike **`e-sewa.com.np`** is **worse still**: cybersquatting that **doubles as a
  phishing trap** (ties forward to Unit 7).

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"First to register a domain owns it fairly."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Registration in **BAD FAITH toward a known trademark is cybersquatting, not
> fair ownership** — the brand can **recover it** via **UDRP (.com)** or the **.np registry.**
> Legitimate domain investing (generic names, no target brand) is a **different, lawful** activity."

> 🍿 **Fun analogy (deliver it):** "Cybersquatting is **grabbing the house-number plate of a famous
> address and demanding ransom for it.** You don't own the house — you just grabbed the sign that
> points to it."

> 🖼️ Visual: a **cybersquatting flow** — register a brand's domain → demand ransom / divert traffic →
> UDRP (.com) or .np dispute resolution.

> 🎙️ Speaker note: Tie forward to Unit 7 phishing — a lookalike `e-sewa.com.np` domain is **both
> cybersquatting and a phishing vector.** Bad faith toward a *known* brand is the deciding factor.

**📊 Depth table — Cybersquatting vs legitimate — and the close cousins**

| Scenario | Verdict | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Registering a Nepali bank's name as .com.np to resell it | Cybersquatting | Bad-faith grab of a known trademark |
| Registering 'e-sewa.com.np' to mimic eSewa | Cybersquatting + phishing risk | Lookalike to divert/deceive users |
| Registering a generic word like 'momos.com' | Legitimate | No target brand; generic term |
| Buying/selling generic domains as investment | Legitimate | Domain investing, no bad-faith target |
| Typosquatting 'gogle.com' to catch typos | Cybersquatting (typosquatting) | Bad-faith lookalike of a known mark |
| Owning your OWN brand's domain | Legitimate | Rightful use of your own mark |

*ℹ️ Bad faith toward a known brand is the deciding factor. .com disputes go to UDRP; .np disputes go through the local registry (Mercantile / register.com.np).*

#### Concept 3 — Pulling It Together: The Four IP Types `[THEORY]` `[~10 min]`

[SLIDE] **Expression · Invention · Secret · Brand**

> **Deliver:** This is the **capstone of the whole unit** — the frame that lets students classify any
> IP question. Put the four types in **one line**:
> - **Copyright** protects **EXPRESSION** — automatic on creation; lasts **life + 50 years.**
> - **Patent** protects an **INVENTION (method)** — applied for and disclosed; **~20 years.**
> - **Trade secret** protects **SECRET information** — kept confidential; **unlimited while secret.**
> - **Trademark** protects **BRAND IDENTITY** — registered and renewed; **potentially forever.**
>
> Each differs in **what it protects, how you acquire it, and how long it lasts.** Then deliver the
> pro insight: **one product can involve all four** — a fintech app's **code is COPYRIGHTED**, a
> **novel mechanism** inside it might be **PATENTED**, its **fraud-scoring algorithm is a TRADE
> SECRET**, and its **name and logo are TRADEMARKED.** Classifying an IP question means asking **which
> of the four — expression, invention, secret, or brand — is at stake.**

- 🇳🇵 **Local example (all four in one):** a Nepali fintech app — **code → copyright** (automatic),
  a **novel mechanism → patent** (if any, rare for software), the **fraud-scoring algorithm → trade
  secret** (NDA-protected, server-side), and the **name + logo → trademark** (register at DoI + grab
  the domain). One product, four protections, four different rules.

> ❌ **Misconception:** *"IP is basically all one thing."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "The four types protect **different things**, are **acquired differently**, and
> **last different lengths of time** — mixing them up gives **wrong answers** (e.g. 'patent your logo'
> or 'copyright your brand name'). Ask **what is at stake**: expression → copyright, invention →
> patent, secret → trade secret, brand → trademark."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: This is the master differentiator of Unit 3. Walk **every row** of the comparison
> table below as the unit's capstone — master this and you can classify any IP question in the exam or
> in a job.

**📊 Depth table — MASTER comparison — the four IP types side by side**

|  | Copyright | Patent | Trade secret | Trademark |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What it protects | Original expression | A new invention (method) | Confidential info | Brand identity (name/logo) |
| How you get it | Automatic on creation | Apply + disclose + 3 tests | Keep it secret (no filing) | Register (DoI) + renew |
| How long it lasts | Life + 50 years | ~20 years | While it stays secret | Indefinite with renewal |
| Then what? | Public domain | Public domain | Gone if leaked/found | Lost if not used/renewed |
| Nepal example | A pirated Kabaddi film | A DoI-registered device | Wai Wai's masala ratio | Fake Goldstar logo |

*ℹ️ Capstone table for Unit 3: the four IP types differ by WHAT they protect, HOW you acquire them, and HOW LONG they last. Master this and you can classify any IP question.*
