## S45 — Cyber Law in Context of Nepal · Legal Perspective of Cybercrime

**Hook:** "Someone makes a fake Facebook/TikTok account in your name, posts defamatory photos, and demands money. Which *police unit* handles this in Nepal, and under *which law* — there's no 'internet' in our old Muluki Ain?" → why a country needs cyber law.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **What is cyber law / cyberspace law**
   - Definition: the body of law governing the use of computers, networks, the internet, and electronic transactions.
   - Theory: it fills the gap where traditional law (written for the physical/paper world) cannot reach digital acts — jurisdiction, evidence, identity, anonymity.
   - Global example: India's IT Act 2000; the Budapest Convention on Cybercrime as a model.
   - Local (Nepal) example: before 2004 Nepal had *no* statute for electronic records; the **Electronic Transaction Act 2063 (2008)** became Nepal's primary cyber law (also called the "Cyber Law" / "Cyber Act").
   - Misconception: *"Nepal has a dedicated 'Cyber Crime Act.'"* Correction: Nepal regulates cybercrime mainly through the **ETA 2063** (an e-transactions law that *also* criminalizes computer offenses), plus provisions in other laws (e.g., defamation, the Criminal Code 2074, Privacy Act 2075).

2. **Why Nepal needs cyber law**
   - Theory: growth of e-banking, e-commerce, e-governance, social media; need to give electronic records/signatures legal validity and to punish digital crime.
   - Local example: rise of mobile banking, eSewa/Khalti wallets, online government services (e.g., online PAN, Nagarik App) — all need a law that makes a digital signature/record legally binding.
   - Mini case: a contract signed and emailed as a PDF — is it enforceable in a Nepali court? (ETA answer: electronic records can have legal recognition.)

3. **Legal perspective of cybercrime — what counts as a cybercrime**
   - Definition: an offense where a computer/network is the *target* or the *tool*.
   - Theory: target crimes (hacking, data theft, damage) vs tool crimes (online fraud, defamation, harassment, child exploitation material).
   - Types recognized in Nepal (under ETA + related law): unauthorized access/hacking, damage to computer source code, electronic forgery, publication of illegal/obscene material electronically, identity theft / fake profiles, online financial fraud, cyber-defamation, cyberbullying/harassment.
   - Local example: **fake social-media accounts and "cyber defamation" cases** are the single largest category handled by the **Nepal Police Cyber Bureau** (established 2018, Bhotahiti, Kathmandu).
   - Mini case: a student edits a friend's photo and posts it to humiliate them — which type of cybercrime, and which body investigates?
   - Fun analogy: cyber law is like traffic law for a brand-new kind of road — the cars (data) move fast and cross borders, so you need new rules and a new traffic police (Cyber Bureau).

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: Nepal's primary cyber law is the…? → Electronic Transaction Act 2063 ✅
- MCQ2: A crime where the computer is the *tool* (not the target) is best illustrated by…? → online fraud / cyber-defamation ✅ (vs hacking, which targets the computer)
- Discussion: "Why couldn't Nepal's traditional laws handle cybercrime — give one concrete reason (identity, evidence, or jurisdiction)."

**Real-life application:** if you are ever impersonated, harassed, or defrauded online in Nepal, you file a complaint with the **Cyber Bureau** (or local police), and the case proceeds under the ETA — knowing this is practical citizenship.

**Summary:** (1) cyber law governs the digital world where old law cannot reach; (2) Nepal's main cyber law is the **ETA 2063**; (3) cybercrime = computer as target or tool, investigated by the **Cyber Bureau**. **Next:** inside the ETA 2063 — its key provisions, digital signatures, and penalties.

**Visual cues:** timeline (no-law era → ETA 2063 → Cyber Bureau 2018 → reform proposals); 2×2 grid "computer as target vs tool"; map pin of the Cyber Bureau.

## S46 — Electronic Transaction Act (ETA) 2063

**Hook:** "What turns a typed-and-emailed agreement into something a Nepali court will enforce — and what turns a curious 'let me just try this login' into a crime with jail time? Same law: the ETA 2063." → the heart of Nepal's cyber law.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **What the ETA 2063 is & its purpose**
   - Definition: the Act that gives **legal recognition to electronic records and digital signatures** and criminalizes computer-related offenses in Nepal.
   - Theory/scope: enacted 2063 BS (2008 AD); organized in chapters covering electronic records & digital signature, dispatch/receipt of records, digital signature certificates, the Controller & Certifying Authorities, network service providers' liability, offenses & penalties, and the IT Tribunal. *(Confirm chapter/section structure against official text.)*
   - Global example: mirrors UNCITRAL Model Law on E-Commerce and India's IT Act in design.
   - Misconception: *"The ETA is only about cybercrime / punishment."* Correction: most of the Act is about *enabling* e-commerce and e-governance (legal validity of e-records/e-signatures); the offenses chapter is only one part.

2. **Legal recognition of electronic records & digital signatures**
   - Definition: an electronic record and a digital signature can carry the same legal validity as paper and handwritten signatures.
   - Theory: digital signature based on asymmetric (public/private) key cryptography; functions = authentication, integrity, non-repudiation.
   - Local example: digital signatures issued under Nepal's PKI framework for secure government/banking transactions; OCR/government e-services accepting digitally signed documents.
   - Mini case: a vendor denies sending a purchase order email — how does a digital signature establish non-repudiation?

3. **Offenses & penalties under the ETA** *(verify all figures)*
   - Key offenses to list: unauthorized access to a computer/network ("hacking"); damage/alteration of computer source code; publication of illegal/obscene/defamatory material in electronic form; breach of confidentiality/privacy; computer fraud and forgery; furnishing false statements to obtain a certificate.
   - Penalties: commonly cited as **fines up to Rs. 200,000 and/or imprisonment up to 5 years** for several core offenses (e.g., the much-cited Section ~47 on publishing illegal material online). **⚠️ Verify the exact section numbers and amounts — these are frequently misquoted.**
   - Local example: **Section 47 of the ETA** has been the most-used (and most-criticized) provision — repeatedly applied to social-media posts, comments, and "defamatory/obscene" content, raising free-speech concerns.
   - Mini case (real-context): individuals arrested over **Facebook/TikTok posts or comments** charged under the ETA's electronic-publication offense — discuss the tension between curbing online harm and protecting free expression.
   - Misconception: *"The ETA clearly defines online defamation and obscenity."* Correction: critics argue Section 47's wording is **vague and over-broad**, which is a core reason behind reform/replacement proposals.
   - Fun analogy: a digital signature is like a wax seal that *also* fingerprints the message — change one word and the seal breaks.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: A digital signature primarily provides authentication, integrity, and…? → non-repudiation ✅
- MCQ2: The most frequently *applied and criticized* provision of the ETA (often used for social-media posts) is…? → Section 47 (publication of illegal material in electronic form) ✅ *(verify number)*
- Discussion: "Should posting a defamatory comment online be treated more seriously than saying it in person? Argue using the ETA."

**Real-life application:** every time you e-sign a document, use mobile banking, or accept online T&Cs in Nepal, the ETA is what makes that legally binding — and what could make a careless post a criminal matter.

**Summary:** (1) ETA 2063 = legal recognition of e-records/e-signatures **plus** computer offenses; (2) digital signatures give authentication/integrity/non-repudiation; (3) the offenses chapter (esp. Section ~47) carries fines + jail and is heavily debated. **Next:** the Rules that operationalize the Act (ETR) and Nepal's IT Policy.

**Visual cues:** ETA "two halves" diagram (Enable e-commerce | Punish cybercrime); digital-signature flow (private key signs → public key verifies); a penalties table placeholder marked "VERIFY FIGURES."

## S47 — Electronic Transaction Rules (ETR) · IT Policy of Nepal

**Hook:** "An Act says 'digital signatures are legal' — but *who* actually issues them, *who* checks the issuer is trustworthy, and *what* national plan pushes Nepal toward a digital economy? That's the Rules and the Policy." → from law on paper to working system.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Electronic Transaction Rules (ETR) 2064**
   - Definition: subordinate legislation made under the ETA that spells out *procedures* — how the Act is implemented in practice.
   - Theory: Rules cover licensing of **Certifying Authorities (CAs)**, procedures for issuing/suspending/revoking **digital signature certificates**, duties of the **Controller**, standards/audit, and fees. *(Confirm year and rule numbers — commonly cited as ETR 2064 BS.)*
   - Local example: licensing requirements a Nepali entity must meet to operate as a Certifying Authority under the Office of the Controller.
   - Misconception: *"Rules and the Act are the same thing."* Correction: the **Act** is the parent law (passed by Parliament); the **Rules** are detailed procedures issued by the executive under the Act and are easier to amend.

2. **Controller of Certification & Certifying Authorities (PKI in Nepal)**
   - Definition: the **Office of the Controller of Certification (OCC / "Controller")** is the government body that licenses and supervises Certifying Authorities; CAs issue digital signature certificates to the public.
   - Theory: trust hierarchy — Controller (root authority) → licensed CAs → end-user certificates; this is Nepal's **PKI** backbone for digital signatures.
   - Local example: the Office of the Controller of Certification (under the Ministry of Communication and Information Technology) and the rollout of licensed CAs to enable legally valid digital signatures in Nepal.
   - Mini case: a CA issues a certificate without verifying identity, and it's used in fraud — what is the Controller's supervisory/audit role?

3. **IT Policy of Nepal**
   - Definition: a national policy document setting the government's vision and goals for ICT development (not a law, but a strategic framework).
   - Theory/goals: build ICT infrastructure, expand e-governance, grow the IT industry/exports and jobs, promote digital literacy and inclusion, and improve information security.
   - Local example: IT Policy goals (e.g., the **IT Policy 2072 / "Digital Nepal Framework"** ambitions) — bridging the digital divide, e-government services, making Nepal a knowledge/IT hub. *(Confirm which policy version is current for teaching.)*
   - Misconception: *"The IT Policy is legally binding like the ETA."* Correction: policy sets *direction and goals*; laws (ETA) and rules (ETR) provide the *binding* legal mechanism.
   - Fun analogy: the **IT Policy** is the destination on the map (where Nepal wants to go digitally), the **ETA** is the road law, and the **ETR + Controller** are the licensing office and traffic police that keep the road usable.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: In Nepal, the body that *licenses and supervises* Certifying Authorities is the…? → Office of the Controller of Certification ✅
- MCQ2: Which is a *strategic framework* rather than binding law? → the IT Policy of Nepal ✅
- Discussion: "Name one IT Policy goal you think Nepal has progressed on, and one it has not — give evidence from daily life."

**Real-life application:** when you obtain a digital signature certificate (for tax, banking, or e-government), it traces back through a licensed CA to the Controller — and national IT Policy goals shape which digital services exist for you at all.

**Summary:** (1) ETR = procedures implementing the ETA (licensing, certificates, Controller's duties); (2) the Controller licenses CAs in Nepal's PKI trust chain; (3) IT Policy sets non-binding national digital goals. **Next:** information security policies — then a full course review and exam/project prep.

**Visual cues:** PKI trust pyramid (Controller → CAs → user certificates); "Act vs Rules vs Policy" comparison table (who makes it / binding? / what it does); Digital Nepal goals iconography.

**Part A — New content: Information Security and Policies (compact)**

1. **Information security & the CIA triad**
   - Definition: protecting information's **Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability**.
   - Theory: link forward/back — this ties Unit 5 (cybersecurity), Unit 6 (personal security), Unit 7 (social engineering) to legal/policy controls here.
   - Local example: a Nepali bank's customer-data protection obligations; NRB/regulatory IT-security expectations.

2. **Information security policy (ISP)**
   - Definition: a documented set of rules governing how an organization protects its information assets.
   - Theory: acceptable-use policy, access control & password policy, data classification, incident response, BYOD, backup/retention; policy → standards → procedures hierarchy.
   - Local example: an organization's acceptable-use and password policy; alignment with Nepal's Privacy Act 2075 / data-protection expectations and the ETA. *(Verify current data-protection status.)*
   - Misconception: *"Security is only a technical/IT-team problem."* Correction: it is governance + people + process; policy and user behavior matter as much as firewalls.
   - Mini case: an employee uses a personal USB / shares a password — which policy should have prevented it, and what's the incident-response step?

3. **Where law meets policy**
   - The ETA criminalizes; the IT Policy directs; **information security policies operationalize** protection inside organizations. Good policy reduces both breaches *and* legal liability.
