## S11 — Intellectual Property Overview · Copyright

**Hook:** "You record a song on your phone, write code for a class app, and design a logo for your dai's momo shop — the moment you create them, who *owns* them, and what stops a stranger from selling them as their own?" → IP is property you can't touch.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Intellectual property (IP)**
   - Definition: legal rights over creations of the mind — works, inventions, names, secrets.
   - Theory: four main families — copyright, patent, trade secret, trademark; protects *expression/invention*, not abstract ideas; balances creator reward vs public access.
   - Global example: Disney's control over Mickey Mouse imagery; the Microsoft Windows codebase.
   - Local example: Nepali IP is administered by the **Department of Industry (DoI)** under the Patent, Design and Trademark Act, 2022 BS (1965); Nepal is a WTO/TRIPS member.
   - Misconception: *"If it's on the internet, it's free to use."* Correction: publication ≠ permission; most online content is still owned.

2. **Copyright — what it protects**
   - Definition: protects original *expression* fixed in a tangible form (text, music, film, software code, art).
   - Theory: protects expression, NOT ideas/facts/methods; arises automatically on creation (no registration needed, though registration helps in disputes); governed in Nepal by the **Copyright Act, 2059 (2002)**, registered at the **Nepal Copyright Registrar's Office**.
   - Global example: source code and a novel are both copyrighted the instant written.
   - Local example: piracy of Nepali films and music — e.g., a movie like *Kabaddi* or songs leaked/copied to YouTube and pirated DVDs in New Road; cracked software CDs.
   - Mini case: "A student copies a Nepali band's melody note-for-note into a college jingle and uploads it — infringement or not?"

3. **Copyright duration & fair use**
   - Definition: copyright lasts a limited term, then the work enters the public domain.
   - Theory: Nepal — author's lifetime + 50 years (works), with shorter/defined terms for some categories; **fair use** allows limited use for education, research, news, criticism, parody without permission.
   - Local example: quoting a few lines of a Nepali poem in a class essay (fair use) vs photocopying a whole textbook for the class (infringement) — the common Bhotahity photocopy-shop dilemma.
   - Misconception: *"Crediting the author makes copying legal."* Correction: attribution avoids plagiarism but does NOT grant copyright permission — separate issues.
   - Fun analogy: copyright is like owning a *recipe written down* — others can cook the dish (idea) but can't photocopy and sell your cookbook (expression).

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: Copyright protects…? → the expression, not the underlying idea ✅
- MCQ2: In Nepal, copyright generally lasts…? → the author's life + 50 years ✅
- Discussion: "Is photocopying an entire textbook to save money fair use? Argue both sides."

**Real-life application:** every project, blog, song, or app you publish is automatically copyrighted — and so is everything you might be tempted to copy.

**Summary:** (1) IP = rights over creations of the mind; (2) copyright protects fixed expression automatically; (3) it expires and has fair-use limits. **Next:** patents — protecting inventions.

**Visual cues:** "4 families of IP" overview graphic (copyright/patent/trade secret/trademark); idea-vs-expression split image; fair-use checklist box.

## S12 — Patent

**Hook:** "Khalti and eSewa both let you pay by phone — could one have *patented* mobile wallets and shut the other down? Why do drug prices and phone features hinge on this question?" → patents grant a temporary monopoly.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Patent — what it is and what is patentable**
   - Definition: an exclusive right granted for an invention — a new, useful, non-obvious product or process — in exchange for public disclosure.
   - Theory: three tests — **novelty, inventiveness (non-obviousness), industrial applicability**; protects the *functional idea*, unlike copyright; typically 20-year term, then public.
   - Global example: pharmaceutical drug patents; the original telephone patent.
   - Local example: patents registered with the **Department of Industry, Nepal** under the Patent, Design and Trademark Act; relatively few local tech patents, mostly designs/agro/herbal products.
   - Misconception: *"You can patent any idea."* Correction: pure ideas, discoveries, and laws of nature are NOT patentable — only concrete, applicable inventions.

2. **Software patents (the debate)**
   - Definition: patents on software-implemented methods/algorithms — controversial worldwide.
   - Theory: copyright covers the *code*; a patent would cover the *method/function* — many jurisdictions (and Nepal in practice) restrict or disallow pure-software patents; risk of "patent trolls."
   - Global example: the Amazon "1-Click" patent; smartphone patent wars (Apple vs Samsung).
   - Local example: a Nepali fintech innovation is usually protected by copyright + trade secret, not patent, given local limits.
   - Mini case: "A Kathmandu startup invents a clever load-balancing algorithm — patent it, keep it secret, or open-source it?"

3. **The patent process**
   - Definition/steps: prior-art search → file application with claims → examination → grant → maintenance/renewal fees.
   - Theory: it is slow, costly, public, and territorial (a Nepal patent doesn't protect you in India).
   - Local example: filing at the DoI vs the cost/effort that pushes startups to file abroad or skip patents.
   - Fun analogy: a patent is like *staking a land claim* — you must publicly mark the boundaries (claims), and after the lease (20 yrs) the land becomes public.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: Which is NOT required for patentability? → being widely popular ✅ (vs novelty/non-obviousness/usefulness)
- MCQ2: A typical patent term is about…? → 20 years ✅
- Discussion: "Should software be patentable in Nepal? What would it do to local startups?"

**Real-life application:** before launching a product you'll do a "freedom-to-operate" check — and decide patent vs secret vs open source.

**Summary:** (1) patents protect novel, useful, non-obvious inventions; (2) software patents are restricted/contested; (3) the process is public, slow, and territorial. **Next:** trade secrets — the patent's silent rival.

**Visual cues:** patentability "3-test" funnel (novel → non-obvious → useful); patent-process timeline; "copyright = code vs patent = method" callout.

## S13 — Trade Secrets

**Hook:** "Wai Wai's exact masala blend, KFC's spice mix, Google's search ranking — none are patented. They're protected by *keeping quiet*. How is silence a legal strategy?" → trade secrets.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Trade secret — definition**
   - Definition: confidential business information that gives a competitive edge and is kept secret through reasonable effort.
   - Theory: covers formulas, recipes, processes, customer lists, algorithms, source code; protection lasts *as long as it stays secret* — potentially forever; no registration.
   - Global example: the Coca-Cola formula; Google's search algorithm.
   - Local example: a noodle/masala brand's spice ratio (Wai Wai / CG Foods); a restaurant's signature recipe; a remit company's pricing model.
   - Misconception: *"A trade secret is the same as a patent."* Correction: a patent is public and time-limited; a trade secret is private and unlimited — but offers no protection if independently discovered.

2. **How trade secrets are protected**
   - Definition: protection via secrecy measures, not government grant.
   - Theory: NDAs, confidentiality clauses in employment contracts, access controls, "need-to-know," non-compete agreements; legal remedy only if secrecy was *misappropriated*.
   - Local example: NDAs in Nepali IT outsourcing/BPO firms; the worry when a developer leaves for a competitor with code knowledge.
   - Mini case: "A developer leaves a Lalitpur software house and rebuilds a similar module from memory at a rival — theft of secret or fair reuse of skill?"

3. **Trade secret vs patent (decision)**
   - Definition: choosing how to protect an invention.
   - Theory: trade secret if hard to reverse-engineer and value is long-term (recipes); patent if it will be sold/visible and reverse-engineerable.
   - Fun analogy: a patent is *shouting your invention from the rooftop in exchange for 20 years of rent*; a trade secret is *whispering it and hoping no one overhears — forever*.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: A trade secret stays protected…? → as long as it remains secret ✅
- MCQ2: The main tool to protect a trade secret with employees is…? → an NDA / confidentiality agreement ✅
- Discussion: "Wai Wai's recipe — patent it or keep it secret? Justify the choice."

**Real-life application:** your first job will likely include an NDA — understanding it protects you and your employer.

**Visual cues:** trade-secret protection layers (NDA → access control → need-to-know); **decision tree** "Patent vs Trade Secret"; running **comparison table** (Copyright | Patent | Trade Secret) — trademark column added in S16.

**Summary:** (1) trade secret = valuable confidential info kept secret; (2) protected by NDAs/controls, not registration; (3) vs patents — private + unlimited but fragile. **Next:** IP *issues* begin — plagiarism and reverse engineering.

## S14 — IP Issues: Plagiarism · Reverse Engineering

**Hook:** "You paste a GitHub function into your project, and your friend pastes a Wikipedia paragraph into a report — both 'borrowed.' Is either illegal? Is either *wrong*?" → plagiarism vs infringement vs legitimate study.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Plagiarism**
   - Definition: presenting someone else's work or ideas as your own, without credit.
   - Theory: an *ethical/academic* offense (distinct from copyright infringement, a *legal* one) — though they often overlap; includes self-plagiarism and AI-generated text passed off as original.
   - Global example: retracted research papers; ChatGPT-written essays submitted as one's own.
   - Local example: copy-paste BIM project reports and thesis sections from seniors/online; **Tribhuvan University** plagiarism/anti-cheating rules; copied news articles in Nepali media.
   - Misconception: *"Changing a few words / citing the source means it's not plagiarism."* Correction: paraphrasing without real understanding or copying structure is still plagiarism; citing avoids it only if the work is genuinely attributed and quoted properly.
   - Fun analogy: plagiarism is wearing someone else's medal to your own race photo.

2. **Reverse engineering**
   - Definition: analyzing a finished product to discover how it was built or how it works.
   - Theory: legal and common for **interoperability, security research, and learning**; crosses the line when used to copy protected code or break a trade secret/contract; EULAs often forbid it.
   - Global example: studying a competitor's chip; security researchers reverse-engineering malware; clean-room re-implementation of an API.
   - Local example: a Nepali developer decompiling a popular app to learn its API, or to clone a UI 1:1 (the line between learning and copying).
   - Mini case: "A team reverse-engineers eSewa's app to build a *compatible* tool vs to clone its features — which is ethical?"

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: Plagiarism is primarily a violation of…? → academic/ethical integrity (not always law) ✅
- MCQ2: Reverse engineering is generally legal when done for…? → interoperability / security research ✅
- Discussion: "Is using AI to write your whole assignment plagiarism? Where's the line?"

**Real-life application:** every report, thesis, and codebase you submit is checked — and security careers depend on *ethical* reverse engineering.

**Summary:** (1) plagiarism = passing off others' work, an integrity offense; (2) reverse engineering is legitimate for learning/security but not for copying; (3) intent and use define the line. **Next:** open source and competitive intelligence.

**Visual cues:** "plagiarism vs copyright infringement" Venn (overlap zone labeled); reverse-engineering "legal use vs misuse" two-column slide.

## S15 — IP Issues: Open Source Code · Competitive Intelligence

**Hook:** "Linux, Android, and most of the internet run on code anyone can copy for free — yet companies make billions on it. And firms legally 'spy' on competitors every day. How is any of this allowed?" → open source licensing + the ethics of intelligence-gathering.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Open source code**
   - Definition: software released under a license that grants rights to use, study, modify, and share the source.
   - Theory: open source ≠ no copyright — it's copyright *plus a license*; **permissive** (MIT, Apache — use freely) vs **copyleft** (GPL — derivatives must also be open); violating the license is infringement.
   - Global example: Linux/Android (GPL); React (MIT); companies sued for shipping GPL code in closed products.
   - Local example: Nepali startups and government projects building on WordPress/Linux/open frameworks to cut cost; the obligation to honor those licenses.
   - Misconception: *"Open source means free and no rules — I can use it however I like."* Correction: licenses impose real obligations (attribution, share-alike); ignoring them is a violation.

2. **Competitive intelligence (ethical vs espionage)**
   - Definition: legally and ethically gathering public information about competitors to inform strategy.
   - Theory: CI uses *public, legal* sources (websites, ads, public filings, job posts, trade shows); it becomes **industrial espionage** when it involves theft, bribery, hacking, misrepresentation, or stealing trade secrets.
   - Global example: analyzing a rival's public annual report (CI) vs hacking their servers (espionage).
   - Local example: a Nepali bank studying competitors' published rates/branches (CI) vs bribing a staffer for a customer list or breaching systems (espionage/crime).
   - Mini case: "A salesperson poses as a customer to extract a rival's secret pricing — CI or espionage?"
   - Fun analogy: CI is *reading the other team's published playbook*; espionage is *breaking into their locker room to steal it*.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: GPL (copyleft) licenses require that…? → derivative works also be released as open source ✅
- MCQ2: Gathering competitor info crosses into espionage when it involves…? → theft, bribery, hacking, or misrepresentation ✅
- Discussion: "Is scraping a competitor's public website for prices ethical competitive intelligence? Where would it cross the line?"

**Real-life application:** you'll ship products built on open source (mind the licenses) and research competitors (stay on the legal side of the line).

**Summary:** (1) open source = copyright + a license with real obligations; (2) permissive vs copyleft; (3) competitive intelligence is legal/public — espionage steals. **Next:** trademarks and cybersquatting close the unit.

**Visual cues:** license spectrum bar (Public Domain → MIT/Apache → GPL → Proprietary); CI-vs-espionage "ethical line" slider with example chips.

## S16 — IP Issues: Trademark Infringement · Cybersquatting (closes Unit 3)

**Hook:** "Someone registers *daraz.com.np* before Daraz does, or sells momos under a logo identical to a famous brand's — they grabbed a name, not code. Is a *name* really property?" → trademarks and domain abuse.

**Concepts & how each will be filled:**

1. **Trademark & trademark infringement**
   - Definition: a trademark is a sign/name/logo/slogan that identifies the *source* of goods or services; infringement is unauthorized use of a confusingly similar mark.
   - Theory: protects brand identity and prevents consumer confusion; can last indefinitely with renewal; registered in Nepal at the **Department of Industry** under the Patent, Design and Trademark Act.
   - Global example: counterfeit "Nike"/"Adidas" goods; lookalike brand names.
   - Local example: fake "Goldstar" shoes, counterfeit "Wai Wai" packets, copycat momo/restaurant logos in Kathmandu; well-known TM disputes filed at the DoI.
   - Misconception: *"Registering a company name means I own the trademark."* Correction: company registration ≠ trademark registration — they are separate filings.

2. **Cybersquatting**
   - Definition: registering a domain name matching someone else's trademark in bad faith — to resell it or divert traffic.
   - Theory: resolved via the **UDRP** (ICANN dispute process) for `.com`, or the local registry for `.np`; distinct from legitimate domain investing; typosquatting is a close cousin.
   - Global example: early disputes over `mcdonalds.com`-type names; brand-name domains held for ransom.
   - Local example: someone registering a `.com.np` domain matching a popular Nepali bank/brand to sell it back, or a lookalike `e-sewa.com.np`-style phishing domain; `.np` domains managed by **Mercantile (register.com.np)**.
   - Mini case: "A person registers a `.com.np` of a famous Nepali brand and offers to sell it for Rs 5 lakh — legitimate business or cybersquatting?"
   - Fun analogy: cybersquatting is *grabbing the house number plate of a famous address and demanding ransom for it*.

3. **Pulling the unit together — the IP comparison**
   - Definition/recap: side-by-side of all four IP types so students can differentiate cleanly.
   - Theory: copyright (expression) | patent (invention) | trade secret (secret info) | trademark (brand identity) — each differs in what it protects, how you get it, and how long it lasts.

**Check for understanding:**
- MCQ1: A logo/brand name that identifies a product's source is protected by…? → trademark ✅
- MCQ2: Registering a brand's domain in bad faith to resell it is…? → cybersquatting ✅
- Discussion: "If you register `famousbrand.com.np` before the brand does, should you be allowed to sell it to them? Argue both sides."

**Real-life application:** naming a startup means a trademark + domain check on day one — get it wrong and you rebrand or face a dispute.

**Summary:** (1) trademarks protect brand identity against confusing use; (2) cybersquatting is bad-faith domain grabbing; (3) the four IP types differ by what/how/how-long. **Next unit:** ethical decisions in software development and IT organizations.

**Visual cues:** **master comparison table** — Copyright vs Patent vs Trade Secret vs Trademark (rows: what it protects · how acquired · duration · example); cybersquatting flow (register → demand ransom → UDRP/.np dispute).
