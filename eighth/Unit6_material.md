### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — What E-Governance Is `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **Using ICT to run and deliver government — counters become code**

> **Deliver:** **E-governance is the use of ICT — websites, apps, databases, networks — by government to
> deliver public services, share information, and interact with citizens more efficiently and
> transparently.** It is the **digitalization of government:** moving service delivery from paper counters
> to digital channels, and **redesigning** the service, not just computerising the old queue.
> - Digitalizes services: forms, records, payments and applications move online.
> - Connects the state to four groups: citizens, businesses, other agencies, and its own staff.
> - Aim is redesigned, accessible service — not merely "computers in the office."
> - Broader than e-government: also covers participation and decision-making (**e-democracy**).

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** **Nagarik App** is e-governance in one place — a citizen views PAN details,
  EPF/CIT balances and vehicle records from a phone instead of visiting several offices. The **online
  passport** system lets you apply and book a slot without camping outside the department at dawn.

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"E-governance just means the government has a website."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "A website is a **channel**; e-governance is **redesigning the service** for digital
> delivery — application, verification, payment and record all online. A scanned paper form you must still
> submit in person is a webpage, not e-governance."

> 🖼️ Visual: the four e-gov types diagram (`s42_egov_types.png`) is introduced in Concept 3.

> 🎯 **Exam-ready answer:** "E-governance is the use of ICT (websites, apps, databases, networks) by
> government to deliver public services, share information and interact with citizens more efficiently and
> transparently — the **digitalization of government** from paper counters to digital channels. It is
> broader than e-government: it also covers citizen **participation** and decision-making. Nepal examples:
> Nagarik App, online passport, IRD e-filing."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: ~7 min. Stress it is redesign, not just scanning old forms.

**📊 Depth table — Traditional service vs e-governed service in Nepal**

| Service | Traditional (before) | E-governed (after) | Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Passport | Queue at the passport dept, forms by hand | Online application + booked appointment | Less time, smaller crowd |
| Electricity bill | Queue at the NEA counter | Pay via eSewa/Khalti/ConnectIPS | Pay anytime, anywhere |
| Vehicle tax (Bluebook) | Queue at transport office, agents | Online / wallet tax payment | Faster, fewer touts |
| PAN / VAT filing | Visit the IRD office | IRD online portal e-filing | Businesses register/file remotely |
| Citizenship/vital records | Ward-office visit, paper ledgers | Nagarik App digital records | Fewer trips to the office |
| Exam results | Notice board / newspaper | Online results portal | Instant, from anywhere |

*ℹ️ E-governance is judged by the 'after' being genuinely easier — not by a scanned PDF of the old form sitting on a website.*

#### Concept 2 — The Four Goals of E-Governance `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **Transparency · Efficiency · Accountability · Participation (T-E-A-P)**

> **Deliver:** **E-governance pursues four goals.** **Transparency:** information and processes are open.
> **Efficiency:** services are faster and cheaper (no queues, less paper). **Accountability:** digital
> records create a trail, so officials can be held answerable. **Participation:** citizens can give
> feedback, complain and take part in decisions.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** Nepal's **e-procurement (PPMO)** shows the goals together — tenders published and
  submitted online (transparency), processed faster (efficiency), every step logged (accountability),
  vendors can query the process (participation). The digital trail makes the old counter-bribe far harder.

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"E-governance is only about doing things faster."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Speed (efficiency) is just **one** of four goals. Transparency, accountability and
> participation matter as much: a fast service that hides its decisions and answers to no one fails the
> point."

> 🧠 **Memory hook:** **T-E-A-P** — Transparency, Efficiency, Accountability, Participation.

> 🎯 **Exam-ready answer:** "E-governance pursues four goals: **transparency** (open information and
> processes), **efficiency** (faster, cheaper, queue-free service), **accountability** (digital records
> create an auditable trail), and **participation** (feedback, grievance and consultation channels). They
> reinforce one another — Nepal's e-procurement delivers all four at once."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: ~7 min. Link each goal to a Nepal example (e-procurement = transparency + accountability).

**📊 Depth table — The four goals — mechanism & Nepal example**

| Goal | How e-governance delivers it | Nepal example |
|---|---|---|
| Transparency | Open data, visible status & processes | PPMO e-procurement tenders published online |
| Efficiency | Automation, self-service, no queues | eSewa/ConnectIPS bill & tax payment |
| Accountability | Every action logged; auditable trail | Digital records make bribes harder to hide |
| Participation | Feedback, grievance & consultation channels | Online grievance portals; ward feedback |

*ℹ️ The four goals reinforce each other: a transparent, logged process is also more accountable, and a self-service one is more efficient. Weakness in one (e.g. no data-protection law) undermines trust in the rest.*

#### Concept 3 — The Four Types: G2C, G2B, G2G, G2E `[THEORY]` `[~8 min]`

[SLIDE] **Classified by WHO the state connects with**

> **Deliver:** E-governance is classified by counterpart. **G2C** (Government-to-Citizen): services and
> information to the public. **G2B** (Government-to-Business): licences, tax, procurement. **G2G**
> (Government-to-Government): data and coordination between agencies and tiers (federal, province, local).
> **G2E** (Government-to-Employee): internal HR, payroll and records. Each flows **both ways.**

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** **Raju** uses **G2C** when he books a passport slot; his shop uses **G2B** when it
  files VAT on the IRD portal and bids on PPMO; the two offices verifying his citizenship use **G2G** when
  they share a registry; and the clerk who serves him is paid through **G2E** payroll. One morning touches
  all four types — which is why **interoperability** (G2G) matters so much.

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"E-governance is only citizens using government websites (G2C)."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "That is the most visible type, but three others matter: **G2B, G2G, G2E.** Much value —
> and many of Nepal's gaps — lie in **G2G:** if agencies don't share data, the citizen re-submits the same
> paper everywhere."

> 🖼️ Visual: `s42_egov_types.png` — the state hub linked both ways to Citizens (G2C), Business (G2B),
> Government (G2G), Employees (G2E).

> 🎯 **Exam-ready answer:** "E-governance is classified by counterpart: **G2C** (services/info to the
> public), **G2B** (licences, tax, procurement), **G2G** (data and coordination across agencies and tiers),
> and **G2E** (internal HR, payroll, records). Each is two-way. Nepal examples: Nagarik App (G2C), IRD
> e-VAT (G2B), the LMBIS budget system (G2G), civil-service payroll (G2E)."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: ~8 min. Use the diagram. Have students place Nagarik App (G2C), IRD e-VAT (G2B), budget
> system (G2G), payroll (G2E).

**📊 Depth table — The four e-governance types — what each connects, Nepal example**

| Type | Connects | What flows | Nepal example |
|---|---|---|---|
| G2C | State <-> Citizens | Services & information to people | Nagarik App, online passport, PAN |
| G2B | State <-> Business | Licences, tax, procurement | IRD e-VAT filing, PPMO e-procurement, OCR company reg. |
| G2G | State <-> Government | Data & coordination across agencies/tiers | Federal-province-local data sharing, LMBIS budget system |
| G2E | State <-> Employees | Internal HR, payroll, records | Civil-service payroll & personnel records |

*ℹ️ Same platform can serve several types: Nagarik App is mainly G2C but touches G2B (business records) and G2G (shared registries) behind the scenes.*

### 📚 CONTENT `[~35 min]`

#### Concept 1 — The Process Model: Input → Process → Output → Feedback `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **A loop, not a one-shot**

> **Deliver:** **E-governance runs on a four-step cycle.** **Input:** the citizen submits a request or data.
> **Process:** the system validates it and applies rules against its databases. **Output:** the service or
> result is delivered. **Feedback:** the citizen rates/complains, or the system logs the outcome — and that
> feeds improvement. It is a **loop.**

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** When **Kanchhi Maiya** renews her scooter tax online — she enters the details
  (input), the system checks records and computes tax + penalty (process), issues a digital receipt
  (output), and she can rate it or file a grievance (feedback). If the department reads that feedback, next
  year's flow gets smoother.

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"An e-service ends when you get your receipt."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "The output is not the end — **feedback** is. Without ratings, complaints and error
> logs, a service cannot improve, and citizens hit the same problem every year."

> 🎯 **Exam-ready answer:** "E-governance follows a four-step cycle: **Input** (citizen submits), **Process**
> (validate and apply rules against databases), **Output** (deliver the result — receipt, approval,
> certificate), and **Feedback** (ratings/grievances/logs close the loop and drive improvement). It is a
> loop, not a one-off; skipping feedback is why services stop improving."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: ~7 min. Emphasise feedback — the step Nepal most often skips.

**📊 Depth table — The process model — each stage with a Nepal example**

| Stage | What happens | Nepal example (paying vehicle tax) |
|---|---|---|
| Input | Citizen submits request/data | Enter Bluebook & vehicle details, choose to pay |
| Process | Validate, apply rules, query registries | System checks records, computes tax + penalty |
| Output | Deliver the service/result | Digital receipt & updated tax status issued |
| Feedback | Rate, complain, log for improvement | Rating/grievance; logs flag repeated errors |

*ℹ️ Skipping feedback is why some Nepali e-services never improve: without ratings and logs feeding back, the same friction repeats every year.*

#### Concept 2 — The 4-Layer Architecture & Security `[THEORY]` `[~8 min]`

[SLIDE] **Presentation / Application / Data / Infrastructure — security wraps all**

> **Deliver:** An e-governance system is built in **four layers.** **Presentation:** the app/website/portal
> you see. **Application:** the business logic — rules, workflows, validation. **Data:** the databases and
> registries (citizen, PAN, vehicle, land). **Infrastructure:** the servers, network and data centre.
> **Security wraps every layer:** National-ID login, encryption, and 2FA/OTP.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** Renewing a passport touches all four layers — the portal (presentation), the
  eligibility rules (application), the citizenship/passport registries (data), the Government Integrated
  Data Centre servers (infrastructure) — while National-ID login, encryption and OTP keep it secure. "The
  site is down" usually means the **infrastructure** layer failed.

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"Security is just one more layer on top."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Security is **not** a layer — it crosses **all four:** login (presentation),
> authorised workflows (application), encrypted registries (data), and hardened servers (infrastructure)."

> 🖼️ Visual: `s43_egov_architecture.png` — the four-layer stack with a security band across all layers.

> 🎯 **Exam-ready answer:** "An e-governance system has four layers: **Presentation** (the interface),
> **Application** (business logic — rules, workflows, validation), **Data** (databases and registries), and
> **Infrastructure** (servers, network, data centre). **Security** — National-ID login, encryption,
> 2FA/OTP — wraps every layer rather than being a separate one. Layering lets one layer change without
> breaking the others."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: ~8 min. Use the stack diagram. 'Server down' = infrastructure layer.

**📊 Depth table — The four architecture layers — role & Nepal example**

| Layer | Role | Nepal example |
|---|---|---|
| Presentation | What the citizen sees & touches | Nagarik App screen; e-passport portal |
| Application | Business logic — rules, workflows, validation | Checks eligibility, computes fees, routes requests |
| Data | Databases & registries that hold records | Citizen, PAN, vehicle, land registries |
| Infrastructure | Servers, network, hosting | Government Integrated Data Centre, fibre, cloud |
| Security (all layers) | Protects every layer | National-ID login, encryption, 2FA/OTP |

*ℹ️ Layering lets one layer change without breaking the others (a new app UI can reuse the same data layer). Security is drawn as crossing all four, not sitting inside one.*

#### Concept 3 — E-Governance Maturity (the UN 4 stages) `[THEORY]` `[~7 min]`

[SLIDE] **Emerging → Enhanced → Transactional → Connected**

> **Deliver:** E-governance grows through **maturity stages.** The UN model has four: **Emerging** (static
> information — read a page), **Enhanced** (downloadable forms, richer content), **Transactional** (do a
> two-way transaction — pay, apply, submit), and **Connected** (services joined across agencies around one
> identity, proactive). Nepal is **reportedly around the transactional stage** for many services, not yet
> connected.

- 🇳🇵 **Local example:** Nepal can pay vehicle tax, apply for a passport and file VAT online (transactional)
  — but the passport office, tax office and citizenship registry run on **separate systems** that don't
  share one identity, so a citizen re-submits documents to each. Reaching **connected** is what National ID
  + G2G data sharing (S44) is meant to unlock.

> ❌ **Common misconception:** *"Once services are online, e-governance is complete."*
> ✅ **Correction:** "Being online (transactional) is stage 3 of 4. The hardest stage is **connected** — one
> identity, agencies sharing data, government acting proactively. Nepal's real gap is joining services up."

> 🎯 **Exam-ready answer:** "E-governance maturity is modelled in four UN stages: **Emerging** (static
> information), **Enhanced** (downloadable forms), **Transactional** (pay/apply/submit fully online), and
> **Connected** (services integrated across agencies around one identity, proactive). Nepal is reportedly
> around **transactional** for many services but **not yet connected** — systems remain siloed, its central
> e-governance gap."

> 🎙️ Speaker note: ~7 min. Nepal has transactional services but they are not 'connected' (siloed).

**📊 Depth table — The four maturity stages — what a citizen can do, and Nepal's position**

| Stage | What the citizen can do | Nepal position (reported) |
|---|---|---|
| Emerging | Read static information on a page | Long past this stage |
| Enhanced | Download forms, richer content | Achieved widely |
| Transactional | Pay, apply, submit fully online | Reached for many services (tax, passport) |
| Connected | One identity, services joined across agencies, proactive | Not yet — systems remain siloed (the main gap) |

*ℹ️ Reported positioning — treat as indicative, not an official score. Nepal's leap from 'transactional' to 'connected' needs shared identity (National ID) and data sharing (G2G interoperability).*
